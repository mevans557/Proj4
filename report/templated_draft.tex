\chapter{Introduction - REDO THIS}

Throughout human history, disease has been one of the greatest societal challenges. From the black death, to COVID-19, widespread infection has disrupted society, caused economic recessions, and caused suffering to millions of people \citep{prabhuHistorysSevenDeadliest}. It is therefore no surprise that Epidemiology has a rich and vaired history, as we have tried to understand diseases from their chemical functions, to their effect on the human body, to how they spread through a population. It is this last problem which this report is concerned with.

Disease modelling has the potential to impact governmental policy with regards to pandemic preparedness and responses, as well as outbreak containment and response \citep{gov.ukEpidemiologicalModellingFrequently}. It is clearly of great importance to develop accurate and realistic models for disease spread, and for these to be flexible enough to be effective in their use \citep{lancetHowModellingCan2024}. It is also important to make these models easy to interpret and communicate to the general public. The effectiveness of this was evident during the COVID-19 pandemic, as a great number of people were encouraged to ``flatten the curve'' - a direct reference to the underlying models used to predict the effect of the pandemic \citep{ecdcVideoCOVID19Flatten2020}.

Now the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic are waning, and so it is important to build upon the huge amount of work that was done at the time, to develop more accurate and useful models for other diseases with different attributes, such as passive immunity from birth. These models can help to shape public policy surrounding not just pandemics and outbreaks, but also vaccination programmes, and other forms of aid globally.

\chapter{The SIS Model}

Here we outline a variety of different approaches to implementing a discrete SI model, consisting of a well-mixed model; a networked discrete metapopulation model (where each node has a population generally greater than $1$); and a networked individuals model (where each node represents an individual, and has a state of either infected or susceptible).

\section{Well-mixed model}

We consider the population to behave like a chemical reaction, and so leverage the mathematical tools relevant to that field here. The reactions we consider are:

\begin{equation}
	\ce{S + I ->[k_I] 2 I} \qquad \ce{I ->[k_S] S}.
\end{equation}

Where $k_I$ is the rate of infection, and $k_S$ the rate of recovery, without immunity. As both reactions contain the same number of reactants, no normalisation for the volume (or, more accurately for disease spread, the population area) is required. We can simulate this reaction system with the following Gillespie SSA algorithm:

\begin{algorithmic}
	\State $r_1 \gets$ \Call{Rand}{}
	\State $r_2 \gets$ \Call{Rand}{}
	\Comment{Get two random numbers uniform in $[0, 1]$}
	\State $\alpha(t) \gets k_I S(t) I(t) + k_S I(t)$
	\Comment{Compute the propensity function}
	\State $\tau \gets \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \log(1/r_1)$
	\Comment{Compute the time until next reaction}
	\If{$r_2 < \frac {k_I S(t) I(t)} {\alpha(t)}$}
		\State $I(t + \tau) = I(t) + 1$
		\State $S(t + \tau) = S(t) - 1$
	\Else
		\State $I(t + \tau) = I(t) - 1$
		\State $S(t + \tau) = S(t) + 1$
	\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}

This allows us to simulate the system, but we also attempt to analyse the system. We can make a simple approximation utilising the law of mass action and treating the populations as continuous functions on time. First, we consider only $I(t)$, the number of infected individuals at time $t$, and take $S(t) = N - I(t)$ the number of susceptible individuals at time $t$ is the total population, minus the number of infected individuals at time $t$. We are also assuming here that the population size does not change over time, so no births or deaths take place over the time period considered. We can then approximate the behaviour of the system with the equation

\begin{equation}
	\fd {I} {t} = k_I (N - I(t)) I(t) - k_S I(t). \label{SIScts}
\end{equation}

This can be solved by separation of variables, as follows

\begin{align}
	\fd {I} {t} &= (k_I N - k_S - k_I I(t)) I(t) \\
	\int dt &= \int \frac {1} {k_I N - k_S - k_I I(t)} \frac {1} {I(t)} \\
	\int dt &= \int \frac {1} {(k_I N - k_S) I(t)} + \frac {k_I} {(k_I N - k_S) ((k_I N - k_S) k_I I(t))} \\
	t + C &= \frac {1} {k_I N - k_S} \big( \ln (I(t)) - \ln ((k_I N - k_S) - k_I I(t)) \big) \\
	\ln \left( \frac {I(t)} {(k_I N - k_S) - k_I I(t)} \right) &= (k_I N - k_S) (t + C) \\
	\frac {(k_I N - k_S) - k_I I(t)} {I(t)} &= A \exp ((k_S - k_I N) t) \\
	k_I N - k_S &= (k_I + A \exp((k_S - k_I N) t)) I(t) \\
	I(t) &= \frac {k_I N - k_S} {k_I + A \exp((k_S - k_I N) t)}.
\end{align}

Setting the initial condition $I(0) = 1$ allows us to specify the constant

\begin{align}
	1 &= \frac {k_I N - k_S} {k_I + A} \\
	A &= k_I N - k_S - k_I.
\end{align}

Giving

\begin{equation}
	I(t) = \frac {N - \frac {k_S} {k_I}} {1 + \left( N - \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 1 \right) \exp((k_S - k_I N)t)}.
\end{equation}

We can also perform a linear stability analysis to predict the expected behaviour of the system. By plotting $\fd {I} {t}$ against $I(t)$, we can see that there is an unstable equilibrium at $I(t) = 0$, the extinction scenario, and a stable equilibrium at $I(t) = N - \frac {k_S} {k_I}$. From this it is clear that with no recovery ($k_S = 0$), the stable equilibrium is total infection of the population. It is also clear that in the (quite extreme) situation of $\frac {k_S} {k_I} = N$, extinction is the only equilibrium, and is stable (as the infected population cannot be negative). However, the mass action model is only an approximation and works best for large numbers of infected individuals.

To gain a more accurate analysis, we leverage the Chemical Master Equation (CME). We begin by considering $P_n (t) = \mathbb{P}(I(t) = n)$. We also have that $P_k (t) \equiv 0$ for $k < 0$ and $k > N$. Then

\begin{align}
	P_n(t + dt) &= k_S (n+1) P_{n+1}(t) dt + k_I (n-1) (N- (n-1)) P_{n-1}(t) dt \\
	&\qquad + P_n(t) (1 - (k_s n + k_I n (N-n)) dt) \\
	\iff \fd {} {t} P_n(t) &= k_S (n+1) P_{n+1}(t) + k_I (n-1) (N- (n-1)) P_{n-1}(t) \\
	&\qquad - (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) P_n(t).
\end{align}

This is not easily solvable analytically, and so we instead examine the mean and variance processes, and the stationary distribution.

We define the mean as $M(t) = \sum_{n = 0}^N n P_n(t)$ and the Variance as $V(t) = \sum_{n = 0}^N (n - M(t))^2 P_n(t)$. We then proceed as follows

\begin{align}
	\fd {M} {t} &= \fd {} {t} \sum_{n = 0}^N n P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^N n \fd {} {t} P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^N n k_S (n+1) P_{n+1} (t) + n k_I (n-1)(N - (n-1)) P_{n-1} (t) - n (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 1}^{N+1} (n-1) k_S n P_n(t) + \sum_{n=-1}^{N-1} (n+1) k_I n (N-n) P_n(t) - \sum_{n=0}^N n (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n=0}^N (n-1) k_S n P_n(t) + (n+1) k_I n (N-n) P_n(t) - n (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n=0}^N k_I n (N-n) P_n(t)-n k_S P_n(t) \\
	&= k_I N \sum_{n=0}^N n P_n(t) - k_S \sum_{n=0}^N n P_n(t) - k_I \sum_{n=0}^N n^2 P_n(t) \\
	&= k_I N M(t) - k_S M(t) - k_I \sum_{n=0}^N n^2 P_n(t). \label{SISmixmean}
\end{align}

Where the change in indices is justified by the factors of $(n \pm 1)$ and $(N-n)$ in the sums, and the definition of $P_n(t)$.

This aligns roughly with what we would expect from the mass action approximation, however the final sum provides some difficulty. Expanding and rearranging the equation for the variance, we get

\begin{equation}
	V(t) = \sum_{n=0}^N n^2 P_n(t) - M(t)^2. \label{variancesquare}
\end{equation}

Therefore, we have that

\begin{equation}
	\fd {M} {t} = k_I(N - M(t)) M(t) - k_S M(t) - k_I V(t).
\end{equation}

So for non-zero variance, we see that this is not the same as the mass action approximation. Unfortunately we can't write a closed-form solution for the mean or variance, as we are just going to get dependence on higher-order terms. In order to sidestep this problem, we attempt to analyse the process using the probability generating function

\begin{equation}
	G: [-1, 1] \times (0, \infty) \mapsto \mathbb{R}: \qquad G(x, t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n P_n(t).
\end{equation}

We can then derive the following:

\begin{align}
	\pd{}{t} \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n P_n(t) &= k_S \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (n+1) x^n P_{n+1}(t) + k_I \sum_{n=0}^\infty (n-1)(N - (n-1)) x^n P_{n-1}(t) \\
	&\qquad - \sum_{n=0}^\infty (k_S + k_I (N - n))n x^n P_n(t).
\end{align}

We note the following:

\begin{align}
	\pd {} {x} G(x, t) &= \sum_{n=1}^\infty n x^{n-1} P_n(t) \\
	\pdd{} {x} G(x, t) &= \sum_{n=2}^\infty n (n-1) x^{n-2} P_n(t).
\end{align}

Changing indices in the above then gives

\begin{align}
	\pd {} {t} \sum_{n = 0}^\infty x^n P_n(t) &= k_S \sum_{n = 1}^\infty n x^{n-1} P_n(t) + k_I \sum_{n = 0}^\infty n (N-n) x^{n+1} P_n(t) \\
	&\qquad - \sum_{n=0}^\infty (k_S + k_I (N-n))n x^n P_n(t) \\
	\pd {} {t} G(x, t) &= k_S \pd {} {x} G(x, t) + k_I (N-1) x^2 \pd {} {x} G(x, t) - k_I x^3 \pdd {} {x} G(x, t) \\
	&\qquad - k_S x \pd {} {x} G(x, t) - k_I N x \pd {} {x} G(x, t) + k_I x^2 \pdd {} {x} G(x, t) + k_I x \pd {} {x} G(x, t) \\
	\pd {} {t} G(x, t) &= (k_S - k_S x + k_I (N-1) (x^2 - x)) \pd {} {x} G(x, t) + (k_I x^2 - k_I x^3) \pdd {} {x} G(x, t).
\end{align}

We can define the initial condition

\begin{equation}
	G(x, 0) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty x^n P_n(0) = x,
\end{equation}

as the starting infected population is $1$. We can also easily get one of the boundary conditions

\begin{equation}
	G(1, t) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty P_n(t) = 1.
\end{equation}

Unfortunately the boundary condition at $x = -1$ is considerably more difficult to derive, as the PDE yields

\begin{equation}
	\pd {} {t} G(-1, t) = (2 k_S + 2 k_I (N-1)) \pd {} {x} G(-1, t) + 2 k_I \pdd {} {x} G(-1, t).
\end{equation}

Clearly to compute this boundary condition, we would already require knowledge of the function on the boundary. We could enforce a Dirichlet boundary condition, and define $G(-1, t)$ to be constant. We might consider that the probability of an odd number of molecules should be approximately equal to the probability of having an even number of molecules, which gives us

\begin{align}
	\frac {G(1, t) + G(-1, t)} {2} &= \sum_{k = 0}^\infty P_{2k}(t) = \frac 1 2\\
	\frac {G(1, t) - G(-1, t)} {2} &= \sum_{k = 0}^\infty P_{2k+1}(t) = \frac 1 2.
\end{align}

This then simply gives that $G(-1, t) = 0$. This also then gives, from the above equation, that the first $x$ derivatives of $G$, evaluated at $-1$, are also $0$.

Attempting to solve this numerically, using tools such as VisualPDE, does not seem to work, potentially due to the complex nature of the equations involved. If we could, this then would give us numerical estimates for the mean and variance of the system, using

\begin{equation}
	\mathbb{E} [I(t)] = \pd {} {x} G(x, t) \Big|_{x = 1} \qquad
	\mathrm{Var} [I(t)] = \left( \pdd {} {x} G(x, t) + \pd {} {x} G(x, t) - \left( \pd {} {x} G(x, t) \right)^2 \right) \Big|_{x = 1}.
\end{equation}

However, due to the difficulty in determining a good boundary condition at $x = -1$, and of then numerically calculating the PGF, we will not do this here, and will instead move onto an analysis of the stationary distribution.

We define the stationary distribution as

\begin{equation}
	\phi(n) := \lim_{t \rightarrow \infty} P_n(t).
\end{equation}

To compute this, we consider the steady-state CME

\begin{equation}
	k_S (n+1) \phi(n+1) + k_I (n-1) (N-(n-1)) \phi(n-1) - (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) \phi(n) = 0.
\end{equation}

Setting $n=0$, we get $k_S \phi(1) = 0$.

We then see, setting $n=1$, that

\begin{equation}
	2 k_S \phi(2) - (k_S + k_I(N-1)) \phi(1) = 0.
\end{equation}

Again this gives $\phi(2) = 0$, from which it is clear that $\phi(k) = 0$ for all $k > 0$. As $\sum_{k=0}^N \phi(k) = 1$, we get $\phi(0) = 1$. This seems initally surprising, although on further consideration, it is clear what is happening here. If at some time $t_0$ we have $I(t_0) = 0$, then we also have $I(t) = 0$ for all $t > t_0$. As we take the limit of time going to infinity, the probability of any given realisation hitting $0$ infected population goes to $1$, and so the stationary distribution takes this form, where only the extinction scenario remains. This is in contrast to the comparable continuous system (\ref{SIScts}), where the extinction scenario is unstable, and the stable equilibrium is one where some fraction of the population is infected. This behaviour is therefore a direct result of the discretised nature of this model, and captures a behaviour not seen in the continuous model.

We can also consider the recovery-free (SI) model, by setting $k_S - 0$. This gives the modified steady-state CME

\begin{equation}
	k_I (n-1)(N-(n-1)) \phi(n-1) - k_I n (N-n) \phi(n) = 0.
\end{equation}

Now setting $n = 0$ clearly gives $\phi(0) = 0$, as we would expect. In fact, it is then clear that all $\phi(n) = 0$, for all $n < N$. For $n = N$, the steady-state CME gives us no information, but again using $\sum_{k = 0}^N \phi(k) = 1$, we see that $\phi(N) = 1$. Again this makes sense, as with no recovery, we expect the whole population to become infected.

The final approach we can consider to obtain more information about the system, is to approximate it as a Stochastic Differential Equation. We define two functions

\begin{equation}
	h_1(n, t) = k_S n P_n(t), \qquad h_2(n, t) = k_I n (N-n) P_n(t).
\end{equation}

These allow us to rewrite the the CME as

\begin{equation}
	\fd {} {t} P_n(t) = h_1 (n+1, t) + h_2 (n-1, t) - h_1 (n, t) - h_2 (n, t).
\end{equation}

Approximating for large $n$, we consider a large fixed $\omega$ such that $n = \eta \omega$, for some continuous $\eta > 0$, and we set $P_n(t) = P(\eta, t)$. We thus have

\begin{equation}
	h_1(\eta, t) = k_S \eta \omega P(\eta, t), \qquad h_2(\eta, t) = k_I \eta \omega (N- \eta \omega) P(\eta, t).
\end{equation}

And the CME is now

\begin{equation}
	\pd {} {t} P(\eta, t) = h_1 (\eta + \frac 1 \omega, t) + h_2 (\eta - \frac 1 \omega, t) - h_1 (\eta, t) - h_2 (\eta, t).
\end{equation}

We then Taylor expand in powers of $\omega$

\begin{align}
	h_1 (\eta + \frac 1 \omega, t) &\approx h_1 (\eta, t) + \frac 1 \omega \pd {} {\eta} h_1 (\eta, t) + \frac 1 {2 \omega^2} \pdd {} {\eta} h_1 (\eta, t) + \mathcal{O} (\frac {h_1} {\omega^3}), \\
	h_2 (\eta - \frac 1 \omega, t) &\approx h_2 (\eta, t) - \frac 1 \omega \pd {} {\eta} h_2 (\eta, t) + \frac 1 {2 \omega^2} \pdd {} {\eta} h_2 (\eta, t) + \mathcal{O} (\frac {h_2} {\omega^3}).
\end{align}

Truncating at $\mathcal{O} (\frac 1 {\omega^2})$ then lets us write

\begin{equation}
	\pd {} {t} P(\eta, t) = \frac 1 \omega \pd {} {\eta} h_1(\eta, t) + \frac {1} {2 \omega^2} \pdd {} {\eta} h_1(\eta, t) - \frac 1 \omega \pd {} {\eta} h_2(\eta, t) + \frac 1 {2 \omega^2} \pdd {} {\eta} h_2(\eta, t).
\end{equation}

We now let $x = \eta \omega$ for ease, so

\begin{equation}
	\pd {} {t} P(x, t) = \pd {} {x} h_1(x, t) + \frac 1 2 \pdd {} {x} h_1(x, t) - \pd {} {x} h_2(x, t) + \frac 1 2 \pdd {} {x} h_2(x, t).
\end{equation}

Where

\begin{equation}
	h_1(x, t) = k_S x P(x, t), \qquad h_2(x, t) = k_I x (N-x) P(x, t).
\end{equation}

So we have

\begin{align}
	\pd {} {t} P(x, t) &= \pd {} {x} \big((k_S x - k_I x (N-x)) P(x, t)\big) + \pdd {} {x} \left(\frac {1} {2}(k_S x + k_I x (N-x)) P(x, t)\right) \\
	&= - \pd {} {x} (f(x) P(x, t)) + \pdd {} x (d(x) P(x, t)).
\end{align}

Where, in the usual Fokker-Planck notation,

\begin{equation}
	f(x) = (k_I x (N-x) - k_S x), \qquad d(x) = \frac {k_S x + k_I x (N-x)} {2} = \frac {g(x)^2} 2.
\end{equation}

This is therefore the Chemical Fokker-Planck equation for this system. We now use the following "computational definition" of SDEs:

\begin{equation}
	X(t + \Delta t) = X(t) + f(X(t)) \Delta t + g(X(t)) \sqrt{\Delta t} \xi.
\end{equation}

Setting the mean $M(t) = \mathbb{E} (X(t))$ and the variance $V(t) = \mathrm{Var} (X(t))$, we derive the following:

\begin{align}
	M(t + \Delta t) &= \mathbb{E} (X(t + \Delta t)) \\
	&= \mathbb{E} (X(t) + f(X(t)) \Delta t + g(X(t)) \sqrt{\Delta t} \xi) \\
	&= \mathbb{E} (X(t)) + \Delta t \mathbb{E} (f(X(t))) + \sqrt{\Delta t}\mathbb{E} (g(X(t))) \mathbb{E} (\xi) \\
	&= M(t) + \Delta t \mathbb{E} (f(X(t))).
\end{align}

So

\begin{align}
	\fd M t &= \mathbb{E} (f(X(t))) \\
	&= \mathbb{E} (k_I X (N-X) - k_S X) \\
	&= (k_I N - k_S) \mathbb{E} (X) - k_I \mathbb{E} (X^2) \\
	&= (k_I N - k_S - k_I M(t)) M(t) - k_I V(t).
\end{align}

Which is the same result we gained from analysing the CME directly, so is unfortunately again intractable.

We can attempt to get an expression for the stationary distribution from the SDE here. Clearly, if we impose the absorbing boundary condition at $x=0$, we will just get the distribution $p_s(x) = 0$, as all trajectories will eventually hit $0$ and be absorbed. However, we are able to extract some useful information by not applying this boundary condition. This will give us a stationary distribution more closely approximating the behaviour we see stopping the simulation after finite time, as the probability of trajectories hitting $0$ in (relatively small) finite time is very low. We use the standard formula

\begin{equation}
	p_s(x) = \frac C {d(x)} \mathrm{exp} \left( \int_0^x \frac {f(y)} {d(y)} dy \right).
\end{equation}

Substituting our functions in gives the following

\begin{align}
	p_s(x) &= \frac {2C} {k_S x + k_I x (N-x)} \mathrm{exp} \left( \int_0^x \frac {2 (k_I y (N-y) - k_S y)} {k_S y + k_I y (N-y)}dy \right) \\
	&= \frac {2C} {k_S x + k_I x (N-x)} \mathrm{exp} \left( \int_0^x 2 - \frac {4k_S} {k_S + k_I (N-y)} dy \right) \\
	&= \frac {2C} {k_S x + k_I x (N-x)} \mathrm{exp} \left( 2x + \frac {4k_S} {k_I} \ln \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right) \right) \\
	&= \frac {2C} {k_S + k_I x (N-x)} e^{2x} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S} {k_I}}.
\end{align}

This is a somewhat complicated function, and difficult to see the behaviour of. Plotting it on an appropriate scale, we can clearly see that it looks similar to a Gaussian, and seems to be centred roughly on the value of $x = N- \frac {k_S} {k_I}$, as we would expect from the equilibrium of the mass action approximation. To quantify this, we can differentiate with respect to $x$, and find the $x$ value of the peak of the distribution.

\begin{align}
	\fd {p_s} x &= - \frac {2C} {x^2 (k_S + k_I (N-x))} e^{2x} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S} {k_I}} \\
	&\qquad + \frac {2C k_I} {x(k_S + k_I (N-x))^2} e^{2x} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S} {k_I}} \\
	&\qquad + \frac {4C} {k_S + k_I x (N-x)} e^{2x} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S} {k_I}} \\
	&\qquad + \frac {2C} {k_S + k_I x (N-x)} e^{2x} \frac {4k_S} {k_I} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S - k_I} {k_I}} \left( - \frac {k_I} {k_S + k_I N} \right) \\
	&= p_s(x) \left[ - \frac 1 x + \frac {k_I} {k_S + k_I (N-x)} + 2 - \frac {4 k_S} {k_S + k_I (N-x)} \right] \\
	&= p_s(x) \left[ \frac {k_I - 4k_S} {k_S + K_I (N-x)} - \frac 1 x + 2 \right].
\end{align}

Clearly we aren't interested about an area where $p_s(x) = 0$, so we only care about where the second part is $0$. Rearranging and applying the quadratic formula gives the (somewhat unweildy) result

\begin{align}
	x &= \frac {2k_S - 2 k_I - 2k_I N \pm \sqrt{4k_S^2 + 4k_I^2 + 4k_I^2 N^2 - 16 k_I k_S - 8 k_I k_S N}} {-4 k_I} \\
	&= \frac N 2 + \frac 1 2 - \frac {k_S} {2 k_I} \mp \frac 1 2 \sqrt{ \left( \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2 + 1 + N^2 - 4 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2 \frac {k_S} {k_I} N}.
\end{align}

While this seems unhelpful, we observe that we can write

\begin{equation}
	N - \frac {k_S} {k_I} = \frac N 2 + \frac 1 2 + \frac {k_S} {2 k_I} - \sqrt{N^2 + 1 + \left( \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2 - 2 N - 2 \frac {k_S} {k_I} N + 2 \frac {k_S} {k_I}}.
\end{equation}

Clearly these expressions are very close together, and we can attempt to quantify the difference. The difference in the expressions under the square root is

\begin{equation}
	6 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2N
\end{equation}

and dividing this by the expression under the square root gives

\begin{align}
	\frac {6 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2N} {\left( N - 1 - \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2} &\approx \frac {6 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2N} {\left(N - \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2} \\
	&= \frac {6 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2N} {N^2 - \frac {k_S} {k_I} N + \left( \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2}.
\end{align}

We then have three possible cases. If $\frac {k_S} {k_I}$ and $N$ are similar in magnitude, then this expression is of magnitude $\frac 1 N \ll 1$. If $\frac {k_S} {k_I}$ is of significantly smaller magnitude than $N$, then the expression is again of magnitude $\frac 1 N \ll 1$. And finally, if $\frac {k_S} {k_I}$ is significantly larger than $N$, then the magnitude of the expression is $\frac {k_I} {k_S} \ll 1$. In all cases, this expression is much smaller than $1$, and as the difference is effectively square rooted, the difference between this peak and the ODE equilibrium $N - \frac {k_S} {k_I}$ should be less than $1$. When we substitute a range of values for $\frac {k_S} {k_I}$ and $N$, this is in fact what we do find. We have therefore shown that we expect, for finite durations of time, for the stochastic simulations to approximate the endemic equilibrium from the mass action approximation, which is what we see.

\section{Metapopulation model}

We consider a graph with: a state vector $\v{I}(t)$, where $I_i(t)$ is the number of infected individuals at node $i$ and at time $t$; adjacency matrix $\m{A}$; and population vector $\v{N}$, where $N_i$ is the number of individuals (in any compartment) at node $i$. We take the population at each node to be fixed over the time interval considered (so there is no birth or death in this model). We also by extension then have the susceptible population in each node at time $t$ as $S_i(t) = N_i - I_i(t)$. If we represent an infected individual in node $i$ as $\ce{I_i}$, and a susceptible individual in node $i$ as $\ce{S_i}$, then we can represent the model with the following system:

\begin{equation}
	\ce{I_i + S_i ->[k_I] 2 I_i} \qquad
	\ce{I_i ->[k_S] S_i} \qquad
	\ce{S_i + I_j ->[A_{ij} k_I] I_i + I_j}.
\end{equation}

Here the first equation corresponds to infection within node $i$, the second to recovery of an infected person in node $i$, and the third infection of an individual in node $i$ by an individual in node $j$. We take the infection and recovery rates $k_I$, $k_S$ to be constant. This corresponds to an assumption that both infection and recovery are purely based on the disease in question, and unaffected by differences between the different metapopulations. We also are imagining the disease spreads across the graph by some interaction between any of the inhabitants of node $i$ with an inhabitant of node $j$.

From these equations, we can see that the mass-action approximation would be of the form

\begin{equation}
	\fd{I_i}{t} = k_I I_i(t) (N_i - I_i(t)) + k_I (N_i - I_i(t)) (\sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t)) - k_S I_i(t).
\end{equation}

If we set $I(t) = \sum_i I_i(t)$ to be the total infected population, and $\sum_i N_i = N$ to be the total population, we get

\begin{align}
	\fd {I} {t} &= k_I \sum_i (I_i(t) N_i) - k_I \sum_i (I_i(t)^2) + k_I \sum_{i, j} A_{ij} I_j(t) N_i - k_I \sum_{i, j} A_{ij} I_j(t) I_i(t) - k_S \sum_i I_i(t) \\
	&= k_I \sum_i (I_i(t) N_i) - k_I \sum_i (I_i(t)^2) + k_I \sum_{j} A_{ij} I_j(t) (N_i - I_i(t)) - k_S I(t) \\
	&= k_I \sum_{i,j} (\m A + \m I)_{ij} I_j(t) (N_i - I_i(t)) - k_S I(t).
\end{align}

This sum is difficult to simplify for a generic graph. However, this equation does bear a strong resemblance to the non-spatial SI equation, but with the spreading term modified by the graph interactions.

We can use the chemical master equations to represent the behaviour of this system as follows:

First, let $p_i(n, t) = \mathbb{P}(I_i(t) = n)$, with $p_i(k, t) = 0$ for $k < 0$ or $k > N_i$. We trivially note that $\sum_{n=0}^{N_i} p_i(n, t) = 1$. Then:

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
	p_i(n, t + \Delta t) &= k_I (N_i - (n-1)) p_i(n-1, t) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) \Delta t \\
	&+ k_I (N_i - (n-1)) (n-1) p_i(n-1, t) \Delta t \\
	&+ k_S (n+1) p_i(n+1, t) \Delta t \\
	&+ \left[ 1 - \left[ k_I (N_i - n) \sum_j A_ij I_j(t) + k_I (N_i - n) n + k_S n \right] \Delta t \right] p_i(n, t).
\end{split}
\end{equation}

By rearranging and taking the limit as $\Delta t \rightarrow 0$, we obtain:

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
	\fd {p_i} {t} (n, t) &= k_I (N_i - (n-1)) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) p_i(n-1, t) + k_I(N_i - (n-1))(n-1) p_i(n-1, t)\\
	&+ k_S (n+1) p_i(n+1, t) - \left(k_I (N_i - n) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) + k_I (N_i - n) n+ k_S n \right) p_i(n, t).
\end{split}
\end{equation}

By setting $M_i(t) = \sum_{n=0}^{N_i} n p_i (n, t)$ to be the average infected individuals at node $i$ at time $t$, we can use the chemical master equations to derive the following:

\begin{align}
	\fd {M_i} {t} &= \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} k_I n (N_i - (n-1)) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) p_i(n-1, t) + n k_I(N_i - (n-1))(n-1) p_i(n-1, t) \notag \\
	&\qquad + n k_S (n+1) p_i(n+1, t) - n \left( k_I (N_i - n) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) + k_I (N_i - n) n + k_S n \right) p_i(n, t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} k_I (n+1) (N_i - n) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) p_i(n, t) + \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} (n+1) k_I (N_i - n) n p_i(n, t)\notag \\
	&\qquad + \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} (n-1) k_S n p_i(n, t) - \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n \left( k_I (N_i - n) \left( \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) + n \right)+ k_S n \right) p_i(n, t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} k_I (N_i - n) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) p_i(n, t) + k_I (N_i - n) n p_i(n, t)- k_S n p_i(n ,t) \\
	&= k_I \left(\sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) \right) \left( \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} N_i p_i(n ,t) - \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n p_i(n, t) \right) \notag \\
	&\qquad + k_I \left(N_i \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n p_i(n, t) - \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n^2 p_i(n, t) \right) - k_S \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n p_i(n, t) \\
	&= k_I \left( \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) \right) (N_i - M_i) + k_I(N_i M_i + M_i^2 - V_i) - k_S M_i. \label{SISmetameans}
\end{align}

Using \ref{variancesquare} in the last step, where $V_i = \sum_{n=0}^{N_i} (n-M_i)^2 p_i(n, t)$. We have also suppressed the index switching, as again this is justified by factors in the sums, and the definition of $p_i(n, t)$. We have also used the fact that, as a probability distribution, $\sum_{n=0}^{N_i} p_i(n, t) \equiv 1$.

We also note that this is really the distribution of $\mathbb{E}(I_i(t) | I_j(t), j \neq i)$, so taking the expectation over $I_j(t)$ as well gives

\begin{equation}
	\fd {M_i} {t} = k_I \left( \sum_j A_{ij} M_j(t) \right) (N_i - M_i) + k_I (N_i M_i + M_i^2 - V_i) - k_S M_i.
\end{equation}

It would often be common to take the mean-field approximation and treat $M_i$ and $M_j$ as independent and identically distributed. However, in this metapopulation model with a small number of nodes, this is not adequately justified.

If we shift our framing slightly, and instead consider an (approximately continuous) "infection density" $\rho_i(t) = \frac {I_i(t)} {N_i}$, we can attempt to analyse equilibria of the system and their stability. The mass-action approximation becomes

\begin{equation}
	\fd {\rho_i} {t} = k_I \rho_i(t) N_i (1- \rho_i(t)) + k_I (1 - \rho_i(t)) \sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j(t) - k_S \rho_i(t).
\end{equation}

Setting the time derivative to $0$ to find the equilibria gives us

\begin{equation}
	k_I N_i \rho_i(t)^2 + (k_I \sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j(t) - k_I N_i + k_S) \rho_i(t) + k_I \sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j(t) = 0.
\end{equation}

We first seek homogeneous solutions to this, of the form $\rho_i = \rho, \forall i$, satisfying

\begin{equation}
	(k_I N_i + k_I \sum_j A_{ij} N_j) \rho^2 + (k_S - k_I N_i + k_I \sum_j A_{ij} N_j) \rho = 0,
\end{equation}

giving $\rho = 0$, the extinction scenario, or

\begin{equation}
	\rho_i = 1 - \frac {k_S} {k_I (N_i + \sum_j A_{ij} N_j)}
	\label{metaHomEq}
\end{equation}

which is highly reminiscent of the $I = N - \frac {k_S} {k_I}$ equilibrium for the well-mixed scenario, and actually is exactly equivalent for the complete graph, as we would expect. As we assumed homogeneity, we can see that we require the population of a node, summed with the populations of its neighbours, must always be constant. For the complete graph, this does not constrain the populations at all, so this solution always exists there. On the cycle graph, it enforces a repeating pattern of three populations, so for cycle graphs with a multiple of $3$ nodes, the population can be varying between nodes, but on any other cycle graph the nodes must all have the same population for this equilibrium to exist. For the path graph, this equilibrium cannot exist, as there is no way for the "end nodes" to have the same population sums as their neighbours, and this is also the case for the star graph.

If we instead allow for inhomogeneous equilibria, we can simplify the equations somewhat by considering constant "neighbour infectivity", i.e. $\sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j = C$, so every node is at the same risk of infection by a neighbouring node as every other node. The equation then becomes

\begin{equation}
	k_I N_i \rho_i^2 + (k_I C - k_I N_i + k_S) \rho_i + k_I C= 0.
\end{equation}

Again the extinction scenario is an equilibrium satisfying constant neighbour infectivity ($C = 0$). The other, endemic equilibria, are given by the complicated expression

\begin{equation}
	\rho_i = \frac {k_I (N_i - C) - k_S \pm \sqrt{k_I^2 (N_i - C)^2 - 2 k_I k_S (N_i - C) + k_S^2 - 4 k_I^2 N_i C}} {2 k_I N_i},
\end{equation}

from the quadratic formula. We will show that these are both positive real roots. It is worth noting the comparison to the well-mixed model, for which there is only a single endemic equilibria.

We can rewrite the roots in a more wieldy manner using the variables $c_i = \frac C {N_i}$ and $k_i = \frac {k_S} {2k_I N_i}$ as

\begin{equation}
	\rho_i = \frac 1 2 (1 - c_i) - k_i \pm \sqrt{\frac 1 4 - \frac {3 c_i} 2 + \frac {c_i^2} {4} - k_i + k_i c_i + k_i^2}.
\end{equation}

Focusing just on the discriminant, and using $k = k_i N_i = \frac {k_S} {2 k_I}$, for real roots we require

\begin{equation}
	\frac {N_i^2} {4} - \frac {3C} {2} N_i + \frac {C^2} {4} - k N_i + k C + k^2 \geq 0.
\end{equation}

This is a positive quadratic in $N_i$, and therefore is satisfied for

\begin{align}
	N_i &\leq 8(\frac {3C} {2} + k - \sqrt{2 k C + 2 C^2}), \\
	N_i &\geq 8(\frac {3 C} {2} + k + \sqrt{2 k C + 2 C^2}).
\end{align}

We can see that both of these roots must be real, as $k, C \geq 0$, and also positive, as

\begin{equation}
	\left( \frac {3 C} {2} + k \right)^2 = \frac 9 4 C^2 + 3 k C + k^2 > 2 k C + 2 C^2.
\end{equation}

In a similar vein, we now return to the equation for $\rho_i$

\begin{align}
	\left( \frac 1 2 (1 - c_i) - k_i \right)^2 &= \frac 1 4 (1 - c_i)^2 - (1- c_i) k_i + k_i^2 \\
	&= \frac 1 4 - \frac {c_i} 2 + \frac {c_i^2} 4 - k_i + c_i k_i + k_i^2 \\
	&\geq \frac 1 4 - \frac {3 c_i} 2 + \frac {c_i^2} 4 - k_i + c_i k_i + k_i^2.
\end{align}

So both $\rho_i \geq 0$, with equality if and only if $c_i = 0$, which gives the extinction equilibrium as expected.

We also have $1 \geq 1 - c_i - 2 k_i \geq \rho_i$, so these are both valid solutions, for some conditions which we now derive.

We note that as $C = \sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j$, we have the vector equation $\v C = \m A (\v N \otimes \boldsymbol{\rho})$, where $\v C$ is the vector where each element is $C$. For our metapopulation model, we have no unconnected vertices, and thus no zero rows or columns in $\m A$, we consider only the case where $\m A$ is invertible. There are no simple conditions for the adjacency matrix of a graph to be invertible, so this process is only applicable to those which do have an invertible adjacency matrix. We can then write $\v N \boldsymbol{\rho} = \m A^{-1} \v C = C \m A^{-1} \v 1$, where $\v 1$ is the vector where each element is $1$. We thus let $\m A^{-1} = \v a$, so we have $\rho_i = \frac {C a_i} {N_i}$. Substituting this into our equation for $\rho_i$ gives

\begin{align}
	\frac {C a_i} {N_i} &= \frac 1 2 (1 - c_i) - k_i \\
	&\qquad \pm \sqrt{\frac 1 4 - \frac {3 c_i} 2 + \frac {c_i^2} {4} - k_i + k_i c_i + k_i^2} \\
	\iff \left( \frac {C a_i} {N_i} + \frac k {N_i} + \frac C {2 N_i} - \frac 1 2 \right)^2 &= \frac 1 4 - \frac {3C} {2N_i} + \frac {C^2} {4 N_i^2} - \frac k {N_i} + \frac {k C} {N_i^2} + \frac {k^2} {N_i^2} \\
	\iff \frac {C^2 a_i^2} {N_i^2} + \frac {2 C k a_i} {N_i^2} + \frac {C^2 a_i} {N_i^2} - \frac {C a_i} {N_i} &= - \frac C {N_i} \\
	\iff \frac {C^2 a_i^2} {N_i} + \frac {2 C k a_i} {N_i} + \frac {C^2 a_i} {N_i} - C a_i + C &= 0 \\
	\iff \left( \frac {a_i^2} {N_i} + \frac {a_i} {N_i} \right) C^2 + \left( \frac {2 k a_i} {N_i} - a_i + \frac 1 {N_i} \right) C &= 0.
\end{align}

This clearly has two solutions. Trivially, $C=0$, giving the extinction equilibrium. Or

\begin{equation}
	C = \frac {a_i N_i - 1 - 2 k a_i} {a_i^2 + a_i}.
\end{equation}

We can rearrange this to give a condition on $N_i$

\begin{equation}
	N_i = \frac {a_i^2 C + a_i C + 2 k a_i + 1} {a_i}.
\end{equation}

This is always positive, so after selecting a $C$ we can calculate the necessary $N_i$ for the equilibrium to exist, and then $\rho_i$ will be determined as above. This can obviously only be performed for graphs with an invertible adjacency matrix, as mentioned earlier.

We can now analyse the stability of these equilibria, following the methods set out in \citep{porterDynamicalSystemsNetworks2015}. In their notation, we have

\begin{align}
	f_i(\rho_i) &= k_I \rho_i(t) N_i (1 - \rho_i(t)) - k_S \rho_i(t), \\
	g_{ij}(\rho_i, \rho_j) &= k_I N_i (1 - \rho_i(t)) \rho_j(t).
\end{align}

We likewise also define

\begin{align}
	a_i &:= k_I N_i (1 - 2 \rho_i^*(t)) - k_S, \\
	b_{ij} &:= -k_I N_i \rho_j^*(t), \\
	c_{ij} &:= k_I N_i (1 - \rho_i^*(t)).
\end{align}

Where $\rho_i^*(t)$ are the equilibria defined above. We then define the matrix $\m M$ as in \citep{porterDynamicalSystemsNetworks2015}

\begin{equation}
	M_{ij} = \delta_{ij} \left[ a_i + \sum_k b_{ik} A_{ik} \right] + c_{ij} A_{ij}.
\end{equation}

Letting the eigenvalues of this matrix be $\mu_r$, we then have the usual stability conditions of local asymptotic stability if $\mathrm{Re}(\mu_r) < 0$ for all $r$, instability if any $\mathrm{Re}(\mu_r)> 0$ and more complex stability if some $\mathrm{Re} (\mu_r) = 0$ and none of the eigenvalues have a positive real part.

For the extinction equilibrium, we have

\begin{equation}
	M_{ij} = k_I N_i A_{ij} + \delta_{ij} (k_I N_i - k_S).
\end{equation}

We focus now on some simple graphs to examine the behaviour of the model. These are the complete graph $k_5$, the cycle graph $C_5$, the path graph $P_5$, and the star graph $S_4$. These are chosen for their simple nature, making them easy to analyse, and their different properties giving us a good intuition for how more complex structures may behave, and they all have the same number of nodes, making them easily comparable. The adjacency matrices are as follows, along with the population vectors required to permit homogeneous equilibria. The matrices $\m M$ for extinction are given by $\m M = k_I \m A + k_I \m N - k_S \m I$ where $\m N$ is the matrix with the populations on the diagonal, and zeroes everywhere else.

For $k_5$ the eigenvalues of the extinction $\m M$ are $k_I N - k_S - k_I$ with degeneracy $4$ and $k_I N - k_S + 3 k_I$ with degeneracy $1$. Both are clearly real, so as $k_I > 0$, this last one is clearly the larger of the two. For stability of the extinction equilibrium we therefore require $N + 3 < \frac {k_S} {k_I}$. This requires either extremely small populations, or a much higher rate of recovery than rate of infection, as we would expect.

For $C_5$ we find the eigenvalues are given by the solutions of the quintic equation

\begin{equation}
	(k_I N - k_I - k_S - \lambda)^5 - 5 k_I^2 (k_I N - k_I - k_S - \lambda)^3 + 4 k_I^4 (k_I N - k_I - k_S - \lambda) + 3 k_I^5 = 0.
\end{equation}

Numerically, we can calculate that the eigenvalues are given by $\lambda = k_I N - k_I - k_S - \gamma k_I$, where $\gamma \in \{ -2.10182, 1.44392, 1.8079, -0.575001 \pm 0.464919i \}$. The eigenvalue with the largest real part is therefore the one corresponding to $\gamma = -2.10182$, giving us the stability condition $\frac {k_S} {k_I} + 1 - N > 2.10182$. In other words, the extinction equilibrium again requires either very small populations or a very large ratio of recovery to infection.

For $P_5$ we again have the eigenvalues given by the solutions of a quintic equation, where if the eigenvalues are $\lambda$ and we write $x = k_I N - k_I - k_S - \lambda$, we have

\begin{equation}
	x^5 - 3 k_I x^4 - k_I^2 x^3 + 4 k_I^3 x^2 - k_I^5 = 0
\end{equation}

which has solutions $x = \gamma k_I$, where $\gamma \in \{-1.13883, -0.459892, 0.510197, 1.38348, 2.70504 \}$. We therefore have $\lambda = (N - 1 - \gamma) k_I - k_S$, so all of the eigenvalues are real, and the largest is given by $(N - 1 + 1.13883) k_I - k_S$, as $k_I > 0$. So for stability of the extinction equilibrium, we require $\frac {k_S} {k_I} > N + 0.13883$. Again, this requires either very small populations or a very large ratio of recovery to infection.

Finally, for $S_4$ the eigenvalues are given by $\lambda = k_I N - k_I - k_S$ with degeneracy 3 and $\lambda = k_I N - k_S - \frac {5 k_I} {2} \pm \frac {k_I} {2} \sqrt{25 - 8 N^2}$. For $N < \frac {5} {2 \sqrt{2}}$ these last lambda are real, and are at most $k_I N - k_S$, so if we have $\frac {k_S} {k_I} > N$, then the extinction equilibrium is stable. If $N$ is larger, the second expression for $\lambda$ is complex, and has real part smaller than $k_I N - k_I - k_S$, so for stability we require $\frac {k_S} {k_I} > N-1$, requiring either small population or large recovery to infection ratio once again.

These changes in the stability of the extinction equilibrium all correspond to a transcritical bifurcation, where the equilibrium at $\rho_i = 0$ always exists, but its stability changes as we vary the system parameters. In fact, the homogeneous equilibrium given by \ref{metaHomEq} drops below $0$ when the equilibrium at $0$ becomes stable. We refer to these regimes from now on as the "supercritical" (unstable $0$ equilibrium) and "subcritical" (stable $0$ equilibrium) regimes.

All simulations started with $20$ individuals infected on node $0$, which is the central node in $S_4$ and one of the end nodes in $P_5$.

\section{Networked individuals model}

To give even more depth to the spatial aspect of this model, we can apply it to a networked population where rather than each node representing a smaller population, each node represents an individual in the overall population. In this kind of model, the precise network structure chosen has a large impact on the behaviour of the model. We focus on a few kinds. Firstly, the complete graph. This should entirely reproduce the results of the well-mixed model, as every individual is in contact with every other individual. Second, we shall consider graphs with low maximum node degree, which may provide sensible models for diseases spread only through specific contact, such as exclusively blood-borne, or sexually-transmitted diseases. These will be the cycle graph, where each individual is connected to exactly $2$ others; and the lattice graph, with each individual connected to (generally) $4$ neighbours. We will also examine how random percolation on this lattice graph may affect the disease spread characteristics, as well as how the disease itself may have percolation-like effects. Finally, we will look at random graphs more closely mimicking social networks, specifically Barabasi-Albert, Watts-Strogatz, and Erdos-Renyi networks, the former being of particular interest due to being a scale-free network. This is a characteristic which is observed to be common to many social networks. The Watts-Strogatz networks demonstrate high clustering, and specifically the small-world property, which are also observed in a variety of real-world networks. These can all be generated easily using the NetworkX package for Python.

Adopting the notation from the metapopulation model, we let $\v{I}$ be the vector storing whether a given node is infected or not, by setting $I_i = 1$ if node $i$ is infected, and $I_i = 0$ otherwise. We set $\m{A}$ to be the adjacency matrix for the graph under examination. We also set the infection rate at $k_I$, and the recovery rate at $k_S$. The chemical equations are

\begin{equation}
	\ce{S_i + I_j ->[A_{ij} k_I] 2 I} \qquad \ce{I_i ->[k_S] S_i}.
\end{equation}

The infection is spread according to the following modified Gillespie SSA:

\begin{algorithmic}
	\State $r_1 \gets$ \Call{Rand}{}.
	\State $r_2 \gets$ \Call{Rand}{}.
	\Comment{Get two random numbers uniform in $[0, 1]$}.
	\State $\alpha(t) \gets k_I (\v{1} - \v{I}(t)) \cdot (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t)) + k_S \sum_i I_i(t)$.
	\Comment{Compute the propensity function}.
	\State $\tau \gets \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \log(1/r_1)$.
	\Comment{Compute the time until next reaction}.
	\If {$r_2 < k_I (\v{1} - \v{I}(t)) \cdot (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))$}
		\State find $j$ such that $r_2 \geq \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \sum_{i = 1}^{j-1} k_I (1 - I_i(t)) (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))_i$ and $r_2 < \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \sum_{i = 1}^j (1 - I_i(t)) (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))_i$.
		Set $I_j(t + \tau) = 1$, and $I_i(t + \tau) = I_i(t)$ for all $i \neq j$.
	\Else
		\State $r_3 \gets r_2 - \frac {k_I} {\alpha(t)} (\v{1} - \v{I}(t)) \cdot (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))$.
		\State find $j$ such that $r_3 \geq \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \sum_{i = 1}^{j-1} k_S I_i(t)$ and $r_3 < \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \sum_{i = 1}^j k_S I_i(t)$.
		Set $I_j(t + \tau) = 0$ and $I_i(t + \tau) = I_i(t)$ for all $i \neq j$.
	\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}

Where $\v{1}$ is the vector where each element is $1$, so that $\v{1} - \v{I}(t)$ is the vector containing a $0$ for each infected node, and a $1$ for each uninfected node. It is then clear that $(\v{1} - \v{I}(t)) * (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))_i$, where $*$ is the component-wise product, is $1$ if the infection could possibly spread to node $i$, and $0$ if not. The above algorithm therefore carries out a single reaction step.

We then define $p_i(t) = \mathbb{P}(I_i(t) = 1)$ to be the probability that node $i$ is infected at time $t$. Considering a small timestep $\Delta t$ then gives use

\begin{equation}
	p_i(t + \Delta t) = (1 - p_i(t)) k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) \Delta(t) + p_i(t) (1 - k_S \Delta t).
\end{equation}

The first term here is the chance of node $i$ being infected in a time step $\Delta t$, with $(1 - p_i(t))$ being the probability of node $i$ being uninfected at time $t$. The chance of infection is also higher if node $i$ has more neighbours. This is a deliberate choice of the model, to represent most social interactions better, where the majority of individuals any given person interacts with have a similar chance of spreading a disease to them. Particularly for the low-degree networks, representing more difficult to spread diseases, it is reasonable to suggest that the interactions between individuals are roughly comparable. The second term in this expression is simply the probability that node $i$ was already infected, and has not recovered in the considered time step.

Taking the limit as $\Delta t \rightarrow 0$ gives

\begin{align}
	\fd {p_i} {t} &= k_I (1 - p_i(t)) \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_S p_i(t) \\
	&= k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - \left( k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) + k_S \right) p_i(t). \label{indvCME}
\end{align}

This can then be rewritten in vector notation

\begin{equation}
	\fd {\v{p}} {t} = k_I \m{A} \v{p}(t) - k_I (\m{A} \v{p}(t)) \odot \v{p}(t) - k_S \v{p}(t).
\end{equation}

Where $\odot$ is the component-wise vector product.

By setting the average number of infected individuals to $M(t) = \sum_{i} p_i(t)$, we can show the following

\begin{align}
	\fd {M} {t} &= \sum_{i} \fd {p_i} {t} \\
	&= \sum_i \left( k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - \left( k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) + k_S \right) p_i(t) \right) \\
	&= \sum_i \left( (1-p_i(t)) k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_S p_i(t) \right) \\
	&= \sum_i (1-p_i(t)) k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_S M(t) \\
	&= k_I \sum_{i, j} (1-p_i(t)) A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_S M(t).
\end{align}

We can take the mean-field limit here, and treat $p_i(t)$ and $p_j(t)$ as independent and identically distributed, giving

\begin{align}
	\fd {M} {t} &= k_I \sum_{ij} A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_I \sum_{ij} A_{ij} p_i(t) p_j(t) - k_S M(t) \\
	&\approx k_I \bar{d(A)} M(t) - k_I \bar{d(A)} \sum_{i} p_i(t)^2 - k_S M(t), 
\end{align}

where $\bar{d(A)}$ is the average degree of the nodes. Then using the identity $V(t) = \sum_{i} p_i(t)^2 - M(t)^2$, we get

\begin{equation}
	\fd {M} {t} \approx k_I \bar{d(A)} M(t) - k_I \bar{d(A)} V(t) - k_I \bar{d(A)} M(t)^2 - k_S M(t).
\end{equation}

This is clearly the same as the equation for the mean in the well-mixed case, with a scaled infection coefficient $k_I \bar{d(A)}$.

It is difficult to directly analyse this system for an arbitrary network, so we will look at only the complete graph, due to its simple adjacency matrix. For this graph, the original equation reduces to

\begin{equation}
	\fd {M} {t} = k_I (N-1) M(t) - k_I \sum_{i \neq j} p_i p_j - k_S M(t)
\end{equation}

This is highly reminiscent of the well-mixed mean (\ref{SISmixmean}), as we would expect, and the similarity of \ref{fig:SISindvcomp} to \ref{fig:SISmix} is apparent.

We can make a brief attempt to find and analyse some equilibria of these systems, by treating the CME probabilities as a continuous approximation of the system, where we can interpret the probabilities approximately as the amount of time we would expect to see a given node infected for. Setting the derivative in the CME \ref{indvCME} to $0$, we get a rather complex expression for the probabilities. To simplify the situation, we consider the mean-field approximation, where we take all $p_i = p$ to be identically and independently distributed. This gives us

\begin{align}
	0 &= k_I p(t) \sum_j A_{ij} - k_I p^2(t) \sum_j A_{ij} - k_S p(t) \\
	&= p(t) \left( k_I \sum_j A_{ij} - k_I p(t) \sum_j A_{ij} - k_S \right).
\end{align}

This clearly shows that $p_i = p = 0$ is an equilibrium of the system, as we would expect. The other possibility is given by $p(t) = 1 - \frac {k_S} {k_I \sum_j A_{ij}}$. The requirement that $p(t)$ are all identical requires that $\sum_j A_{ij}$ is the same for all $i$. This is satisfied, for example, by several kinds of tree graph, the complete graph, the cycle graph, and (in the infinite limit) the $2$D lattice. We again see that in the case of the complete graph, we reproduce behaviour very close to that of the well-mixed model, as if we sum over the $p_i$, we get $M = N - \frac {k_S N} {k_I (N-1)}$. Analysing the stability of these equilibria is unfortunately very difficult, as we would be required to find the eigenvalues of an $N \times N$ matrix, where $N$ is in general very large. However, as the behaviour is not dissimilar to the well-mixed and metapopulation models, we could expect to see some kind of critical point at which the extinction equilibrium becomes stable.

We now simulate the infection on a variety of graph structures. We begin with a few simple types of graph, in order to demonstrate some important effects. These are the Cycle graph, Tadpole graph, Lollipop graph, and Barbell graph. We then move on to some more complex random-generated graphs, these being the Erdos-Renyi, Barabasi-Albert, and Watts-Strogatz. We then simulate on a small selection of percolation-type lattices, with differing percolation coefficients.

These different kinds of random graphs exhibit a variety of interesting properties, but all also have limitations compared to real-life examples of social networks. The Erdos-Renyi model exhibits low clustering, which is not the case with real social networks (if person $A$ knows people $B$ and $C$, then it is generally likely that person $B$ knows person $C$). The Barabasi-Albert model and Watts-Strogatz model don't suffer from this problem, but have their own shortcomings. The Watts-Strogatz model does exhibit high clustering, as well as the small-world property (including short average path lengths, the famous "seven degrees of separation" idea), but is not scale-free, which social networks are thought to be. This is a shortcoming that the Barabasi-Albert model seeks to address, however it too does not demonstrate the small-world property. A further abstraction is made in the percolation-type networks, where every individual can only be connected to a maximum of $4$ others. This isn't highly realistic for most diseases, though could be the case for diseases spread only through specific forms of contact (such as STIs or blood-borne diseases), which most individuals only experience with a small number of others. The percolation graphs demonstrate a strong difference in behaviour based on how well-connected the network is as a whole, which may be useful in understanding the spread of diseases such as HIV, HPV, or hepatitis. We also see very clearly from these different simulations that the specifics of a given graph have a very strong impact on the disease characteristics.
