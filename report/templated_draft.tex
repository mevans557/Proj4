\chapter{Introduction and Background}

Throughout human history, disease has been a constant threat to health and happiness. From the black death sweeping through Europe from 1347 to 1353, killing an estimated 50 million, to the recent COVID-19 pandemic which shut down world travel and trade while killing in excess of 25 million \citep{dattaniWhatWereDeath2023}, the impacts of disease on society can be vast. It is therefore of great importance to develop accurate models for the spread of different kinds of diseases, to understand how they work, and how best to combat their spread. In the recent COVID-19 pandemic, many countries based their responses on mathematical models \citep{smithHowHaveMathematical2023}, and this has led to these models occupying a much greater space in the public consciousness. Before the pandemic, most people had probably never heard of the R number $R_0$, but during the pandemic, discussion around this modelling parameter permeated a large amount of the news \citep{bbcCoronavirusWhatNumber2020}. Developing good models is therefore extremely important for pandemic preparedness \citep{lancetHowModellingCan2024}.

The original work in the field of mathematical disease modelling was done in 1927, by Kermack and McKendrick \citep{kermackContributionMathematicalTheory1927}, where they introduced the concept of a compartmental model, in which the population of concern is split into several compartments. They primarily examined what became known as the SIR model, where individuals are considered to either be Susceptible, Infected, or Removed (either through recovery or death). In this model, only Susceptible people can be infected, and only Infected people can be removed - there is also no movement back into the Susceptible compartment. Once an individual has had the disease, they are either dead, or immune for life. This model is applicable to a large number of diseases that were of great concern at the time, such as measels, polio, or diptheria. However, in the present day UK, where most people are vaccinated against these diseases \citep{ukhsaVaccinationCoverageStatistics}, we are more interested in diseases from which we only develop temporary immunity, or those from which no immunity is gained at all. In this project, we focus on the related, but more simple SIS model, where individuals are either Susceptible or Infected. Like in the SIR model, only Susceptible individuals can be infected, but we also allow infected individuals to recover, without immunity, and become Susceptible again. This is a good model for several common diseases against which no immunity is developed, such as Gonorrhea or Syphillis. This model is also a good starting point to develop the machinery for a more complex future model, as it can easily be extended to have more compartments, or to introduce the effects of social distancing and other measures, as in \citet{croccoloSpreadingInfectionsRandom2020}.

Another departure we make from the original work of Kermack and McKendrick is to consider discrete populations, where they considered the populations to be continuous. This means we can no longer use the machinery of ODEs that they utilise, and so we must choose another method of modelling this discrete process. A natural way to do this is by treating the individuals in different compartments as different chemical species, and the transitions between compartments as chemical reactions. This allows us to leverage a large amount of machinery developed for these kinds of discrete stochastic processes.

We first focus on a model with no spatial variation, this is equivalent to thinking of our individuals (which we are treating as chemicals) as being well-mixed, and so we refer to this as the well-mixed model. We then examine two ways of introducing spatial structure using networks. First, we analyse the system on simple graphs, with a relatively large population on each node. This is known as the metapopulation model, and each of the nodes can be thought of as representing a town or village in this model, which are themselves well-mixed. Second, we look at several models for networks of individuals, where each node of the network represents an individual person. This is referred to as the individual model. Finally, we look at the behaviour of the model on a real social network, utilising the SNAP dataset \citep{snapnets} to simulate the individual model on a network of Facebok users and their friends.

For all of these models, we examine the stability of the equilibria of ODE approximations of the models, and show that there exists a tipping point for all of them, at which the extinction equilibrium becomes stable.

\chapter{Well-mixed model}

\section{The Gillespie SSA and Mass-Action Approximation}

Here we examine a discretised SIS model without any network structure, where all individuals are assumed to be in contact with all others. This will allow us to develop the tools we will need to analyse the networked models, and will provide a point of comparison for the networked models.
We consider the population to behave like a chemical reaction, and so leverage the mathematical tools relevant to that field here. The reactions we consider are:

\begin{equation}
	\ce{S + I ->[k_I] 2 I} \qquad \ce{I ->[k_S] S}.
\end{equation}

Where $k_I$ is the rate of infection, and $k_S$ the rate of recovery, without immunity. As both reactions contain the same number of reactants, no normalisation for the volume (or, more accurately for disease spread, the population area) is required. We can simulate this reaction system with the following Gillespie SSA algorithm:

\begin{algorithmic}
	\State $r_1 \gets$ \Call{Rand}{}
	\State $r_2 \gets$ \Call{Rand}{}
	\Comment{Generate two random numbers uniform in $[0, 1]$}
	\State $\alpha(t) \gets k_I S(t) I(t) + k_S I(t)$
	\Comment{Compute the propensity function}
	\State $\tau \gets \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \log(1/r_1)$
	\Comment{Compute the time until next reaction}
	\If{$r_2 < \frac {k_I S(t) I(t)} {\alpha(t)}$}
		\State $I(t + \tau) = I(t) + 1$
		\State $S(t + \tau) = S(t) - 1$
	\Else
		\State $I(t + \tau) = I(t) - 1$
		\State $S(t + \tau) = S(t) + 1$
	\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}

This is an algorithm, originally implemented by David Kendall, but popularised by Dan Gillespie. It allows us to simulate a Markov jump process, such as this one. This process satisfies the Markov property, as the chance of a reaction occuring depends purely on the current numbers of different chemicals in the system, and not on the system's past. We can thus use this algorithm to simulate our system, though we also attempt several other forms of analysis. We can make a simple approximation utilising the law of mass action and treating the populations as continuous functions on time. First, we consider only $I(t)$, the number of infected individuals at time $t$, and take $S(t) = N - I(t)$ the number of susceptible individuals at time $t$ is the total population, minus the number of infected individuals at time $t$. We are also assuming here that the population size does not change over time, so no births or deaths take place over the time period considered. We can then approximate the behaviour of the system with the equation

\begin{equation}
	\fd {I} {t} = k_I (N - I(t)) I(t) - k_S I(t). \label{SIScts}
\end{equation}

This can be solved by separation of variables, to get

\begin{equation}
	I(t) = \frac {k_I N - k_S} {k_I + A \mathrm{exp}((k_S - k_I N) t)}.
\end{equation}

Setting the initial condition $I(0) = 1$ allows us to specify the constant

\begin{align}
	1 &= \frac {k_I N - k_S} {k_I + A} \notag \\
	\implies A &= k_I N - k_S - k_I.
\end{align}

Giving

\begin{equation}
	I(t) = \frac {N - \frac {k_S} {k_I}} {1 + \left( N - \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 1 \right) \exp((k_S - k_I N)t)}. \label{WMmassAct}
\end{equation}

We can also perform a linear stability analysis to predict the expected behaviour of the system. By plotting $\fd {I} {t}$ against $I(t)$, we can see that there is an unstable equilibrium at $I(t) = 0$, the extinction scenario, and a stable equilibrium at $I(t) = N - \frac {k_S} {k_I}$. From this it is clear that with no recovery ($k_S = 0$), the stable equilibrium is total infection of the population. It is also clear that in the (quite extreme) situation of $\frac {k_S} {k_I} = N$, extinction is the only equilibrium, and is stable (as the infected population cannot be negative). However, the mass action model is only an approximation and works best for large numbers of infected individuals.

\section{The Chemical Master Equation and Probability Generating Function}

To gain a more accurate analysis, we leverage the Chemical Master Equation (CME). We begin by considering $P_n (t) = \mathbb{P}(I(t) = n)$, the probability that at time $t$ there are $n$ infected individuals. We therefore have that $P_k (t) \equiv 0$ for $k < 0$ and $k > N$. Then

\begin{multline}
	P_n(t + dt) = k_S (n+1) P_{n+1}(t) dt + k_I (n-1) (N- (n-1)) P_{n-1}(t) dt \\
	+ P_n(t) (1 - (k_s n + k_I n (N-n)) dt).
\end{multline}

Now taking the limit as $dt \rightarrow 0$, we have

\begin{multline}
	\fd {} {t} P_n(t) = k_S (n+1) P_{n+1}(t) + k_I (n-1) (N- (n-1)) P_{n-1}(t) \\
	 - (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) P_n(t).
\end{multline}

This is not easily solvable analytically, as it is a system of $N$ equations (where $N$ is large), and so we instead examine the mean and variance processes, and the stationary distribution.

\subsection{Mean and Variance} \label{MeanVar}

We define the mean as $M(t) = \sum_{n = 0}^N n P_n(t)$ and the Variance as $V(t) = \sum_{n = 0}^N (n - M(t))^2 P_n(t)$. We then proceed as follows

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\fd {M} {t} &= \fd {} {t} \sum_{n = 0}^N n P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^N n \fd {} {t} P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^N n k_S (n+1) P_{n+1} (t) + n k_I (n-1)(N - (n-1)) P_{n-1} (t) - n (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 1}^{N+1} (n-1) k_S n P_n(t) + \sum_{n=-1}^{N-1} (n+1) k_I n (N-n) P_n(t) - \sum_{n=0}^N n (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n=0}^N (n-1) k_S n P_n(t) + (n+1) k_I n (N-n) P_n(t) - n (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n=0}^N k_I n (N-n) P_n(t)-n k_S P_n(t) \\
	&= k_I N \sum_{n=0}^N n P_n(t) - k_S \sum_{n=0}^N n P_n(t) - k_I \sum_{n=0}^N n^2 P_n(t) \\
	&= k_I N M(t) - k_S M(t) - k_I \sum_{n=0}^N n^2 P_n(t). \label{SISmixmean}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Where the change in indices is justified by the factors of $(n \pm 1)$ and $(N-n)$ in the sums, and the definition of $P_n(t)$.

This is visually very similar to the ODE for the mass action approximation, however the final sum provides some difficulty. Expanding and rearranging the equation for the variance, we get

\begin{equation}
	V(t) = \sum_{n=0}^N n^2 P_n(t) - M(t)^2. \label{variancesquare}
\end{equation}

Therefore, we have that

\begin{equation}
	\fd {M} {t} = k_I(N - M(t)) M(t) - k_S M(t) - k_I V(t).
\end{equation}

So for non-zero variance, we see that this is not the same as the mass action approximation. Unfortunately we cannot write a closed-form solution for the mean or variance without making some sort of closure, as we are just going to get dependence on higher-order terms. The simplest closure would be to just take the variance to be zero, however this just gives the mass-action approximation. We therefore take a moment closure \citep{kuehnMomentClosureBrief2016} where we assume that any higher-order moments are zero. Defining the third centralised moment as $J(t) = \sum_{n = 0}^N (n-M(t))^3 P_n(t) = 0$ and expanding gives

\begin{equation}
	\sum_{n = 0}^N n^3 P_n(t) = 3 M(t) V(t) + M(t)^3,
\end{equation}

which allows us to simplify the ODE for variance

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\fd{V}{t} &= \sum_{n = 0}^N n^2 \fd {P_n(t)(t)}{t} \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^N n^2 k_S (n+1) P_{n+1}(t) + n^2 k_I (n-1) (N - (n-1)) P_{n-1} (t) - n^2 (k_S n + k_I n (N - n)) P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^N (n-1)^2 k_S n P_n(t) + (n+1)^2 k_I n (N-n) P_n(t) - n^2 (k_S n + k_I n (N - n)) P_n(t) \\
	&= \sum_{n=0}^N \left[ k_S n - 2 k_S n^2 + 2 k_I N n^2  + k_I N n - 2 k_I n^3 - k_I n^2 \right] P_n(t) \\
	&= (k_S + k_I N) M(t) + (2k_I N - 2k_S - k_I) (V(t) + M(t)^2)  - 6 k_I M(t) V(t) - 2 k_I M(t)^3.
\end{aligned}	
\end{equation}

Where we have used the closure to write everything in terms of the mean and variance, and where the change in indices in justified as for the mean.

These two equations now constitute a system of coupled nonlinear ODEs. This is very difficult to solve analytically, so we instead solve the system numerically, using the fourth order Runge-Kutta algorithm \citep{cheeverFourthOrderRungeKutta}. However, when we attempt to do this, we find that the numerical solution blows up. The issue here is that we always have positive $\fd V t$. This shows us that this closure is not sufficient to capture the behaviour of the system accurately. We therefore try some different approaches to analyse the behaviour of the system not already captured by the mass action approximation.

\subsection{Probability Generating Function}

We can leverage the probability generating function to try to gain some insight into the behaviour of this system. This is defined as

\begin{equation}
	G: [-1, 1] \times (0, \infty) \mapsto \mathbb{R}: \qquad G(x, t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n P_n(t). \label{PGFdef}
\end{equation}

And we can recover the moments for the system using the formulae

\begin{equation}
	\mathbb{E} [I(t)] = \pd {} {x} G(x, t) \Big|_{x = 1} \qquad
	\mathrm{Var} [I(t)] = \left( \pdd {} {x} G(x, t) + \pd {} {x} G(x, t) - \left( \pd {} {x} G(x, t) \right)^2 \right) \Big|_{x = 1}.
	\label{PGFident}
\end{equation}

From the definition \ref{PGFdef} we can derive:

\begin{multline}
	\pd{}{t} \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n P_n(t) = k_S \sum_{n = 0}^\infty (n+1) x^n P_{n+1}(t) + k_I \sum_{n=0}^\infty (n-1)(N - (n-1)) x^n P_{n-1}(t) \label{PGFCME} \\
	- \sum_{n=0}^\infty (k_S + k_I (N - n))n x^n P_n(t).
\end{multline}

We note the following useful identities:

\begin{align}
	\pd {} {x} G(x, t) &= \sum_{n=1}^\infty n x^{n-1} P_n(t), \\
	\pdd{} {x} G(x, t) &= \sum_{n=2}^\infty n (n-1) x^{n-2} P_n(t).
\end{align}

Changing indices in \ref{PGFCME} then gives the PDE

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\pd {} {t} \sum_{n = 0}^\infty x^n P_n(t) &= k_S \sum_{n = 1}^\infty n x^{n-1} P_n(t) + k_I \sum_{n = 0}^\infty n (N-n) x^{n+1} P_n(t) \\
	&\qquad - \sum_{n=0}^\infty (k_S + k_I (N-n))n x^n P_n(t) \\
	\implies \pd {} {t} G(x, t) &= k_S \pd {} {x} G(x, t) + k_I (N-1) x^2 \pd {} {x} G(x, t) - k_I x^3 \pdd {} {x} G(x, t) \\
	&\qquad - k_S x \pd {} {x} G(x, t) - k_I N x \pd {} {x} G(x, t) + k_I x^2 \pdd {} {x} G(x, t) + k_I x \pd {} {x} G(x, t) \\
	\implies \pd {} {t} G(x, t) &= (k_S - k_S x + k_I (N-1) (x^2 - x)) \pd {} {x} G(x, t) + (k_I x^2 - k_I x^3) \pdd {} {x} G(x, t).
\end{aligned}
	\label{PGFPDE}
\end{equation}

We can define the initial condition

\begin{equation}
	G(x, 0) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty x^n P_n(0) = x^{n_0},
\end{equation}

where the initial number of infected individuals is $n_0$, so $P_n(0) = 0$ for all $n \neq n_0$, and $P_{n_0}(0) = 1$. We can also see one of the boundary conditions

\begin{equation}
	G(1, t) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty P_n(t) = 1,
\end{equation}

which follows immediately from the normalisation of the probabilities.

Unfortunately the boundary condition at $x = -1$ is considerably more difficult to derive, as the PDE yields

\begin{equation}
	\pd {} {t} G(-1, t) = (2 k_S + 2 k_I (N-1)) \pd {} {x} G(-1, t) + 2 k_I \pdd {} {x} G(-1, t).
\end{equation}

Clearly to compute this boundary condition, we would already require knowledge of the function on the boundary. We could enforce a Dirichlet boundary condition, and define $G(-1, t)$ to be constant, setting $G(-1, t) = (-1)^{n_0}$ for consistency with the initial condition above. We might also consider that the probability of an odd number of molecules should be approximately equal to the probability of having an even number of molecules, which gives us

\begin{align}
	\frac {G(1, t) + G(-1, t)} {2} &= \sum_{k = 0}^\infty P_{2k}(t) = \frac 1 2\\
	\frac {G(1, t) - G(-1, t)} {2} &= \sum_{k = 0}^\infty P_{2k+1}(t) = \frac 1 2.
\end{align}

This then simply gives that $G(-1, t) = 0$. This also then gives, from the above equation, that the first $x$ derivatives of $G$, evaluated at $-1$, are also $0$. However, this is not consistent with the initial condition, so we instead opt for the Dirichlet condition that $G(-1, t) = (-1)^{n_0}$.

We can solve this system numerically, using VisualPDE \citep{walkerVisualPDERapidInteractive2023}. Doing this allows us to use the earlier identities \ref{PGFident} to get numerical estimates for the mean and variance of the system over time.

\begin{equation}
	\mathbb{E} [I(t)] = \pd {} {x} G(x, t) \Big|_{x = 1} \qquad
	\mathrm{Var} [I(t)] = \left( \pdd {} {x} G(x, t) + \pd {} {x} G(x, t) - \left( \pd {} {x} G(x, t) \right)^2 \right) \Big|_{x = 1}.
\end{equation}

We in fact see in the numerical solution, tracking these expressions over time, that the mean rises as we would expect, before levelling off. We also see the variance drop rapidly to a very low value, showing that we expect the solutions to rapidly converge towards an equilibrium. This is an excellent result, as we do not get the blowing up that we had when trying to use the moment closure earlier.

\section{The Stationary Distribution}

We are most interested in the long-term behaviour of the system, and whether the disease will die out or not over time. We therefore examine the stationary distribution, defined as

\begin{equation}
	\phi(n) := \lim_{t \rightarrow \infty} P_n(t).
\end{equation}

From \citep{pollettProblemEstablishingExistence1993}, we know that the stationary distribution for a continuous-time Markov Process exists if the corresponding transition rate matrix is irreducible and regular. As this is the case for the process we are examining, we can move ahead with actually computing this distribution.
To begin, we consider the steady-state CME

\begin{equation}
	k_S (n+1) \phi(n+1) + k_I (n-1) (N-(n-1)) \phi(n-1) - (k_S n + k_I n (N-n)) \phi(n) = 0.
\end{equation}

Setting $n=0$, we get $k_S \phi(1) = 0$.

We then see, setting $n=1$, that

\begin{equation}
	2 k_S \phi(2) - (k_S + k_I(N-1)) \phi(1) = 0.
\end{equation}

Again this gives $\phi(2) = 0$, from which it is clear that $\phi(k) = 0$ for all $k > 0$. As $\sum_{k=0}^N \phi(k) = 1$, we get $\phi(0) = 1$. This seems initally surprising, although on further consideration, it is clear what is happening here. If at some time $t_0$ we have $I(t_0) = 0$, then we also have $I(t) = 0$ for all $t > t_0$. As we take the limit of time going to infinity, the probability of any given realisation hitting $0$ infected population goes to $1$, and so the stationary distribution takes this form, where only the extinction scenario remains. This is in contrast to the comparable continuous system (\ref{SIScts}), where the extinction scenario is unstable, and the stable equilibrium is one where some fraction of the population is infected. This behaviour is therefore a direct result of the discretised nature of this model, and captures a behaviour not seen in the continuous model.

We can also consider the recovery-free (SI) model, by setting $k_S - 0$. This gives the modified steady-state CME

\begin{equation}
	k_I (n-1)(N-(n-1)) \phi(n-1) - k_I n (N-n) \phi(n) = 0.
\end{equation}

Now setting $n = 0$ gives $\phi(0) = 0$, as we would expect. In fact, it follows that all $\phi(n) = 0$, for all $n < N$. For $n = N$, the steady-state CME gives us no information, but again using $\sum_{k = 0}^N \phi(k) = 1$, we see that $\phi(N) = 1$. Again this makes sense, as with no recovery, we expect the whole population to become infected.

\section{Stochastic Differential Equation Approach, and Finite-Time Stationary Distribution Approximation}

The final approach we can consider to obtain more information about the system, is to approximate it as a Stochastic Differential Equation, following the procedure laid out in \citet{erbanStochasticDifferentialEquations2020}. We define two functions

\begin{equation}
	h_1(n, t) = k_S n P_n(t), \qquad h_2(n, t) = k_I n (N-n) P_n(t).
\end{equation}

These allow us to rewrite the the CME as

\begin{equation}
	\fd {} {t} P_n(t) = h_1 (n+1, t) + h_2 (n-1, t) - h_1 (n, t) - h_2 (n, t).
\end{equation}

Approximating for large $n$, we consider a large fixed $\omega$ such that $n = \eta \omega$, for some continuous $\eta > 0$, and we set $P_n(t) = P(\eta, t)$. This continuous approximation lets us write

\begin{equation}
	h_1(\eta, t) = k_S \eta \omega P(\eta, t), \qquad h_2(\eta, t) = k_I \eta \omega (N- \eta \omega) P(\eta, t).
\end{equation}

And the CME is now

\begin{equation}
	\pd {} {t} P(\eta, t) = h_1 (\eta + \frac 1 \omega, t) + h_2 (\eta - \frac 1 \omega, t) - h_1 (\eta, t) - h_2 (\eta, t).
\end{equation}

We then Taylor expand in powers of $\omega$

\begin{align}
	h_1 (\eta + \frac 1 \omega, t) &\approx h_1 (\eta, t) + \frac 1 \omega \pd {} {\eta} h_1 (\eta, t) + \frac 1 {2 \omega^2} \pdd {} {\eta} h_1 (\eta, t) + \mathcal{O} \left(\frac {h_1} {\omega^3}\right), \\
	h_2 (\eta - \frac 1 \omega, t) &\approx h_2 (\eta, t) - \frac 1 \omega \pd {} {\eta} h_2 (\eta, t) + \frac 1 {2 \omega^2} \pdd {} {\eta} h_2 (\eta, t) + \mathcal{O} \left(\frac {h_2} {\omega^3}\right).
\end{align}

Where $\mathcal {O} (n)$ indicates terms of order $n$ or higher ($n, n^2$, etc).

Truncating at $\mathcal{O} (\frac 1 {\omega^2})$ then lets us write

\begin{equation}
	\pd {} {t} P(\eta, t) = \frac 1 \omega \pd {} {\eta} h_1(\eta, t) + \frac {1} {2 \omega^2} \pdd {} {\eta} h_1(\eta, t) - \frac 1 \omega \pd {} {\eta} h_2(\eta, t) + \frac 1 {2 \omega^2} \pdd {} {\eta} h_2(\eta, t).
\end{equation}

We now let $x = \eta \omega$ for ease, so

\begin{equation}
	\pd {} {t} P(x, t) = \pd {} {x} h_1(x, t) + \frac 1 2 \pdd {} {x} h_1(x, t) - \pd {} {x} h_2(x, t) + \frac 1 2 \pdd {} {x} h_2(x, t).
\end{equation}

Where

\begin{equation}
	h_1(x, t) = k_S x P(x, t), \qquad h_2(x, t) = k_I x (N-x) P(x, t).
\end{equation}

So we have

\begin{align}
	\pd {} {t} P(x, t) &= \pd {} {x} \big((k_S x - k_I x (N-x)) P(x, t)\big) + \pdd {} {x} \left(\frac {1} {2}(k_S x + k_I x (N-x)) P(x, t)\right) \notag \\
	&= - \pd {} {x} (f(x) P(x, t)) + \pdd {} x (d(x) P(x, t)). \label{ChemFPEq}
\end{align}

This is a PDE known as the Chemical Fokker Planck equation. We then have the drift and diffusion terms

\begin{equation}
	f(x) = (k_I x (N-x) - k_S x), \qquad d(x) = \frac {k_S x + k_I x (N-x)} {2} = \frac {g(x)^2} 2.
\end{equation}

We now use the following "computational definition" of SDEs:

\begin{equation}
	X(t + \Delta t) = X(t) + f(X(t)) \Delta t + g(X(t)) \sqrt{\Delta t} \xi
\end{equation}

where $\xi \sim N(0, 1)$ is normally distributed with $0$ mean and a variance of $1$. We can then iterate in time steps of $\Delta t$, taking a new $\xi$ each time, according to the normal distribution - this accounts for the noise in the process.

Setting the mean $M(t) = \mathbb{E} (X(t))$ and the variance $V(t) = \mathrm{Var} (X(t))$, we derive the following:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	M(t + \Delta t) &= \mathbb{E} (X(t + \Delta t)) \\
	&= \mathbb{E} (X(t) + f(X(t)) \Delta t + g(X(t)) \sqrt{\Delta t} \xi) \\
	&= \mathbb{E} (X(t)) + \Delta t \mathbb{E} (f(X(t))) + \sqrt{\Delta t}\mathbb{E} (g(X(t))) \mathbb{E} (\xi) \\
	&= M(t) + \Delta t \mathbb{E} (f(X(t))).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

So

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\fd M t &= \mathbb{E} (f(X(t))) \\
	&= \mathbb{E} (k_I X (N-X) - k_S X) \\
	&= (k_I N - k_S) \mathbb{E} (X) - k_I \mathbb{E} (X^2) \\
	&= (k_I N - k_S - k_I M(t)) M(t) - k_I V(t).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Which is the same result we gained from analysing the CME directly, and so this would give the same results as in \ref{MeanVar}

We can attempt to get an expression for the stationary distribution from the SDE here. If we impose the absorbing boundary condition at $x=0$, we will just get the distribution $p_s(x) = 0$, as all trajectories will eventually hit $0$ and be absorbed. However, we are able to extract some useful information by not applying this boundary condition. This will give us a stationary distribution more closely approximating the behaviour we see stopping the simulation after finite time, as the probability of trajectories hitting $0$ in (relatively small) finite time is very low. By setting the time derivative in \ref{ChemFPEq} to $0$, we can derive the formula for the stationary distribution

\begin{multline}
	\fdd{}{x} \left( \frac {g(x)^2} {2} p_s(x) \right) - \fd{}{x} \left( f(x) p_s(x) \right) = 0 \\
	\implies \fd{}{x} \left( \frac {g(x)^2} {2} p_s(x) \right) - f(x) p_s(x) = C = 0 \quad \text{WLOG} \\
	\implies \left[ g(x) g'(x) - f(x) \right] p_s(x) + \frac {g(x)^2} {2} p_s'(x) = 0 \\
	\implies \fd{}{x} p_s(x) + \left[ \frac {2 g'(x)} {g(x)} - \frac {f(x)} {g(x)^2} \right] p_s(x) = 0 \\
	\implies g(x)^2 \mathrm{exp} \left(- \int_0^x \frac {2 f(y)} {g(y)^2} dy \right) \fd{}{x} p_s(x) + \left[ 2 g'(x)g(x) - 2 f(x) \right] \mathrm{exp} \left(- \int_0^x \frac {2 f(y)} {g(y)^2} dy \right) p_s(x) = 0 \\
	\implies \fd{}{x} \left( g(x)^2 \mathrm{exp} \left(- \int_0^x \frac {2 f(y)} {g(y)^2} dy \right) p_s(x) \right) = 0 \\
	\implies g(x)^2 \mathrm{exp} \left( - \int_0^x \frac{2 f(y)} {g(y)^2} dy \right) p_s(x) = 2C.
\end{multline}

Giving us

\begin{equation}
	p_s(x) = \frac C {d(x)} \mathrm{exp} \left( \int_0^x \frac {f(y)} {d(y)} dy \right).
\end{equation}

Substituting our functions for $f, g$ in gives the following

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	p_s(x) &= \frac {2C} {k_S x + k_I x (N-x)} \mathrm{exp} \left( \int_0^x \frac {2 (k_I y (N-y) - k_S y)} {k_S y + k_I y (N-y)}dy \right) \\
	&= \frac {2C} {k_S x + k_I x (N-x)} \mathrm{exp} \left( \int_0^x 2 - \frac {4k_S} {k_S + k_I (N-y)} dy \right) \\
	&= \frac {2C} {k_S x + k_I x (N-x)} \mathrm{exp} \left( 2x + \frac {4k_S} {k_I} \ln \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right) \right) \\
	&= \frac {2C} {k_S + k_I x (N-x)} e^{2x} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S} {k_I}}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

This is a somewhat complicated function, and difficult to intuit the behaviour of. Plotting it on an appropriate scale, we can see that it looks similar to a Gaussian, and seems to be centred roughly on the value of $x = N- \frac {k_S} {k_I}$, as we would expect from the equilibrium of the mass action approximation. To quantify this, we can differentiate with respect to $x$, and find the $x$ value of the peak of the distribution.

\begin{figure}
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/SDEStatcomp.png}
	\caption{A comparison of the stationary distribution from this SDE (purple) to a Gaussian centred on $N - \frac {k_S} {k_I}$ (red). For this plot, $N = 400$, $k_I = 0.02$, $k_S = 2$. It is clear that these are extremely close, and the peak of the SDE-derived solution is very close to the equilibrium of the mass-action approximation given by \ref{WMmassAct}}
	\label{fig:SDEstatcomp}
\end{figure}

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\fd {p_s} x &= - \frac {2C} {x^2 (k_S + k_I (N-x))} e^{2x} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S} {k_I}} \\
	&\qquad + \frac {2C k_I} {x(k_S + k_I (N-x))^2} e^{2x} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S} {k_I}} \\
	&\qquad + \frac {4C} {k_S + k_I x (N-x)} e^{2x} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S} {k_I}} \\
	&\qquad + \frac {2C} {k_S + k_I x (N-x)} e^{2x} \frac {4k_S} {k_I} \left( 1 - \frac {k_I x} {k_S + k_I N} \right)^{\frac {4k_S - k_I} {k_I}} \left( - \frac {k_I} {k_S + k_I N} \right) \\
	&= p_s(x) \left[ - \frac 1 x + \frac {k_I} {k_S + k_I (N-x)} + 2 - \frac {4 k_S} {k_S + k_I (N-x)} \right] \\
	&= p_s(x) \left[ \frac {k_I - 4k_S} {k_S + K_I (N-x)} - \frac 1 x + 2 \right].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Clearly we are not interested about an area where $p_s(x) = 0$, so we only care about where the second part is $0$. Rearranging and applying the quadratic formula gives the (somewhat unweildy) result

\begin{align}
	x &= \frac {2k_S - 2 k_I - 2k_I N \pm \sqrt{4k_S^2 + 4k_I^2 + 4k_I^2 N^2 - 16 k_I k_S - 8 k_I k_S N}} {-4 k_I} \notag \\
	&= \frac N 2 + \frac 1 2 - \frac {k_S} {2 k_I} \mp \frac 1 2 \sqrt{ \left( \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2 + 1 + N^2 - 4 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2 \frac {k_S} {k_I} N}.
\end{align}

While this does not offer much insight in itself, we observe that we can write

\begin{equation}
	N - \frac {k_S} {k_I} = \frac N 2 + \frac 1 2 - \frac {k_S} {2 k_I} - \frac {1} {2} \sqrt{N^2 + 1 + \left( \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2 - 2 N - 2 \frac {k_S} {k_I} N + 2 \frac {k_S} {k_I}}. \label{FPStatapprox}
\end{equation}

These expressions are very similar, and we can attempt to quantify the difference. The difference in the expressions under the square root is

\begin{equation}
	6 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2N
\end{equation}

and dividing this by the expression under the square root in \ref{FPStatapprox} gives

\begin{align}
	\frac {6 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2N} {\left( N - 1 - \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2} &\approx \frac {6 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2N} {\left(N - \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2} \notag \\
	&= \frac {6 \frac {k_S} {k_I} - 2N} {N^2 - \frac {k_S} {k_I} N + \left( \frac {k_S} {k_I} \right)^2}.
\end{align}

We then have three possible cases. If $\frac {k_S} {k_I}$ and $N$ are similar in magnitude, then this expression is of magnitude $\frac 1 N \ll 1$. If $\frac {k_S} {k_I}$ is of significantly smaller magnitude than $N$, then the expression is again of magnitude $\frac 1 N \ll 1$. And finally, if $\frac {k_S} {k_I}$ is significantly larger than $N$, then the magnitude of the expression is $\frac {k_I} {k_S} \ll 1$. In all cases, this expression is much smaller than $1$, and as the difference is effectively square rooted, the difference between this peak and the ODE equilibrium $N - \frac {k_S} {k_I}$ should be less than $1$. When we substitute a range of values for $\frac {k_S} {k_I}$ and $N$, this is in fact what we do find. We have therefore shown that we expect, for finite durations of time, for the stochastic simulations to approximate the endemic equilibrium from the mass action approximation, which is what we see.

We have now developed several tools for analysing this model, specifically the Chemical Master Equations, mass action approximation, and stochastic differential equations. We also analysed the probability generating function for the chemical master equations. We have used these to determine a stationary distribution for the simulations, and to analyse the stability of solutions to the mass action approximation, which we have used to predict the qualitative behaviour of the model. We have seen that the model results in noisy trajectories, which are approximated by logistic growth, in the case that the extinction equilibrium of the mass action ODE is unstable, and which decay exponentially to extinction, in the case that the extinction equilibrium of the mass action ODE is stable. In the discussion on network models, we will focus primarily on the chemical master equations, approximations of their solutions, and the resulting stability analysis.

\chapter{Metapopulation model}

\section{Motivation}

Having now developed a series of tools for the non-spatial model, we examine the simplest kind of spatially-structured model, the metapopulation model. In this model, we introduce a graph structure, where each node of the graph is considered to be a smaller well-mixed population, and so the dynamics are that of the well-mixed model within each smaller population, with the addition of infection from neighbouring nodes. These smaller populations can be considered to represent small communities, such as villages or small towns, where the population interacts with eachother regularly, but also has close interaction with neighbouring villages. As a result of this framework, we choose to model the interaction between nodes as if each person in node A has contact with every person in node B, and so the infection risk of a person in node A is related to the total number of infected people in node B, rather than the fraction of infected people in node B. We draw attention to this assumption, as most other models instead consider the infection risk to be proportional to the fraction of infected people in node B, and our choice to take this other approach has significant impacts on the model behaviour and our analysis.

\section{Setup and Chemical Master Equations}

We consider a graph with: a state vector $\v{I}(t)$, where $I_i(t)$ is the number of infected individuals at node $i$ and at time $t$; adjacency matrix $\m{A}$; and population vector $\v{N}$, where $N_i$ is the number of individuals (in any compartment) at node $i$. We take the population at each node to be fixed over the time interval considered (so there is no birth or death in this model). We also by extension then have the susceptible population in each node at time $t$ as $S_i(t) = N_i - I_i(t)$. If we represent an infected individual in node $i$ as $\ce{I_i}$, and a susceptible individual in node $i$ as $\ce{S_i}$, then we can represent the model with the following system:

\begin{equation}
	\ce{I_i + S_i ->[k_I] 2 I_i} \qquad
	\ce{I_i ->[k_S] S_i} \qquad
	\ce{S_i + I_j ->[A_{ij} k_I] I_i + I_j}.
\end{equation}

Here the first equation corresponds to infection within node $i$, the second to recovery of an infected person in node $i$, and the third infection of an individual in node $i$ by an individual in node $j$. We take the infection and recovery rates $k_I$, $k_S$ to be constant. This corresponds to an assumption that both infection and recovery are purely based on the disease in question, and unaffected by differences between the different metapopulations. We also are imagining the disease spreads across the graph by some interaction between any of the inhabitants of node $i$ with an inhabitant of node $j$.

From these equations, we can see that the mass-action approximation would be of the form

\begin{equation}
	\fd{I_i}{t} = k_I I_i(t) (N_i - I_i(t)) + k_I (N_i - I_i(t)) (\sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t)) - k_S I_i(t). \label{metaMA}
\end{equation}

If we set $I(t) = \sum_i I_i(t)$ to be the total infected population, and $\sum_i N_i = N$ to be the total population, we get

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\fd {I} {t} &= k_I \sum_i (I_i(t) N_i) - k_I \sum_i (I_i(t)^2) + k_I \sum_{i, j} A_{ij} I_j(t) N_i - k_I \sum_{i, j} A_{ij} I_j(t) I_i(t) - k_S \sum_i I_i(t) \\
	&= k_I \sum_i (I_i(t) N_i) - k_I \sum_i (I_i(t)^2) + k_I \sum_{j} A_{ij} I_j(t) (N_i - I_i(t)) - k_S I(t) \\
	&= k_I \sum_{i,j} (\m A + \mathbb{I})_{ij} I_j(t) (N_i - I_i(t)) - k_S I(t)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbb{I}$ is the identity matrix.

This sum is difficult to simplify for a generic graph. However, this equation does bear a strong resemblance to the non-spatial SIS equation, but with the spreading term modified by the graph interactions.

We can use the chemical master equations to represent the behaviour of this system as follows:

First, let $p_i(n, t) = \mathbb{P}(I_i(t) = n)$, with $p_i(k, t) = 0$ for $k < 0$ or $k > N_i$. We trivially note that $\sum_{n=0}^{N_i} p_i(n, t) = 1$. Then:

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
	p_i(n, t + \Delta t) &= k_I (N_i - (n-1)) p_i(n-1, t) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) \Delta t \\
	&+ k_I (N_i - (n-1)) (n-1) p_i(n-1, t) \Delta t \\
	&+ k_S (n+1) p_i(n+1, t) \Delta t \\
	&+ \left[ 1 - \left[ k_I (N_i - n) \sum_j A_ij I_j(t) + k_I (N_i - n) n + k_S n \right] \Delta t \right] p_i(n, t).
\end{split}
\end{equation}

By rearranging and taking the limit as $\Delta t \rightarrow 0$, we obtain:

\begin{multline}
	\fd {p_i} {t} (n, t) = k_I (N_i - (n-1)) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) p_i(n-1, t) + k_I(N_i - (n-1))(n-1) p_i(n-1, t)\\
	+ k_S (n+1) p_i(n+1, t) - \left(k_I (N_i - n) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) + k_I (N_i - n) n+ k_S n \right) p_i(n, t).
\end{multline}

By setting $M_i(t) = \sum_{n=0}^{N_i} n p_i (n, t)$ to be the average infected individuals at node $i$ at time $t$, we can use the chemical master equations to derive the following:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\fd {M_i} {t} &= \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} k_I n (N_i - (n-1)) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) p_i(n-1, t) + n k_I(N_i - (n-1))(n-1) p_i(n-1, t) \notag \\
	&\qquad + n k_S (n+1) p_i(n+1, t) - n \left( k_I (N_i - n) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) + k_I (N_i - n) n + k_S n \right) p_i(n, t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} k_I (n+1) (N_i - n) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) p_i(n, t) + \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} (n+1) k_I (N_i - n) n p_i(n, t)\notag \\
	&\qquad + \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} (n-1) k_S n p_i(n, t) - \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n \left( k_I (N_i - n) \left( \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) + n \right)+ k_S n \right) p_i(n, t) \\
	&= \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} k_I (N_i - n) \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) p_i(n, t) + k_I (N_i - n) n p_i(n, t)- k_S n p_i(n ,t) \\
	&= k_I \left(\sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) \right) \left( \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} N_i p_i(n ,t) - \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n p_i(n, t) \right) \notag \\
	&\qquad + k_I \left(N_i \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n p_i(n, t) - \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n^2 p_i(n, t) \right) - k_S \sum_{n = 0}^{N_i} n p_i(n, t) \\
	&= k_I \left( \sum_j A_{ij} I_j(t) \right) (N_i - M_i) + k_I(N_i M_i + M_i^2 - V_i) - k_S M_i. \label{SISmetameans}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Using \ref{variancesquare} in the last step, where $V_i = \sum_{n=0}^{N_i} (n-M_i)^2 p_i(n, t)$. We have also suppressed the index switching, as again this is justified by factors in the sums, and the definition of $p_i(n, t)$. We have also used the fact that, as a probability distribution, $\sum_{n=0}^{N_i} p_i(n, t) \equiv 1$.

We also note that this is really the distribution of $\mathbb{E}(I_i(t) | I_j(t), j \neq i)$, so taking the expectation over $I_j(t)$ as well gives

\begin{equation}
	\fd {M_i} {t} = k_I \left( \sum_j A_{ij} M_j(t) \right) (N_i - M_i) + k_I (N_i M_i + M_i^2 - V_i) - k_S M_i.
\end{equation}

It would often be common to take the mean-field approximation and treat $M_i$ and $M_j$ as independent and identically distributed. However, in this metapopulation model with a small number of nodes, the network structure cannot be considered to "average out" in the correct manner, and so this is not a valid assumption. As a result, we instead aim to make a different continuous approximation of the system, to analyse its stability.

\section{Continuous Approximation, Equilibria, and Stability}

If we shift our framing slightly, and instead consider an (approximately continuous) "infection density" $\rho_i(t) = \frac {I_i(t)} {N_i}$, we can attempt to analyse equilibria of the system and their stability. The mass-action approximation \ref{metaMA} becomes

\begin{equation}
	\fd {\rho_i} {t} = k_I \rho_i(t) N_i (1- \rho_i(t)) + k_I (1 - \rho_i(t)) \sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j(t) - k_S \rho_i(t).
\end{equation}

Setting the time derivative to $0$ to find the equilibria gives us

\begin{equation}
	k_I N_i \rho_i(t)^2 + (k_I \sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j(t) - k_I N_i + k_S) \rho_i(t) + k_I \sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j(t) = 0.
\end{equation}

We first seek homogeneous solutions to this, of the form $\rho_i = \rho, \forall i$, satisfying

\begin{equation}
	(k_I N_i + k_I \sum_j A_{ij} N_j) \rho^2 + (k_S - k_I N_i + k_I \sum_j A_{ij} N_j) \rho = 0,
\end{equation}

giving $\rho = 0$, the extinction scenario, or

\begin{equation}
	\rho_i = 1 - \frac {k_S} {k_I (N_i + \sum_j A_{ij} N_j)}
	\label{metaHomEq}
\end{equation}

which is highly reminiscent of the $I = N - \frac {k_S} {k_I}$ equilibrium for the well-mixed scenario, and actually is exactly equivalent for the complete graph, as we would expect. As we assumed homogeneity, we can see that we require the population of a node, summed with the populations of its neighbours, must always be constant. For the complete graph, this does not constrain the populations at all, so this solution always exists there. On the cycle graph, it enforces a repeating pattern of three populations, so for cycle graphs with a multiple of $3$ nodes, the population can be varying between nodes, but on any other cycle graph the nodes must all have the same population for this equilibrium to exist. For the path graph, this equilibrium cannot exist, as there is no way for the "end nodes" to have the same population sums as their neighbours, and this is also the case for the star graph.

If we instead allow for inhomogeneous equilibria, we can simplify the equations somewhat by considering constant "neighbour infectivity", i.e. $\sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j = C$, so every node is at the same risk of infection by a neighbouring node as every other node. The equation then becomes

\begin{equation}
	k_I N_i \rho_i^2 + (k_I C - k_I N_i + k_S) \rho_i + k_I C= 0.
\end{equation}

Again the extinction scenario is an equilibrium satisfying constant neighbour infectivity ($C = 0$). The other, endemic equilibria, are given by the complicated expression

\begin{equation}
	\rho_i = \frac {k_I (N_i - C) - k_S \pm \sqrt{k_I^2 (N_i - C)^2 - 2 k_I k_S (N_i - C) + k_S^2 - 4 k_I^2 N_i C}} {2 k_I N_i},
\end{equation}

from the quadratic formula. We will show that these are both positive real roots. It is worth noting the comparison to the well-mixed model, for which there is only a single endemic equilibria.

We can simplify the notation using the variables $c_i = \frac C {N_i}$ and $k_i = \frac {k_S} {2k_I N_i}$ as

\begin{equation}
	\rho_i = \frac 1 2 (1 - c_i) - k_i \pm \sqrt{\frac 1 4 - \frac {3 c_i} 2 + \frac {c_i^2} {4} - k_i + k_i c_i + k_i^2}.
\end{equation}

Focusing just on the discriminant, and using $k = k_i N_i = \frac {k_S} {2 k_I}$, for real roots we require

\begin{equation}
	\frac {N_i^2} {4} - \frac {3C} {2} N_i + \frac {C^2} {4} - k N_i + k C + k^2 \geq 0.
\end{equation}

This is a positive quadratic in $N_i$, and therefore is satisfied for

\begin{align}
	N_i &\leq 8 \left(\frac {3C} {2} + k - \sqrt{2 k C + 2 C^2}\right), \quad \text{or} \\
	N_i &\geq 8 \left(\frac {3 C} {2} + k + \sqrt{2 k C + 2 C^2}\right).
\end{align}

We can see that both of these roots must be real, as $k, C \geq 0$, and also positive, as

\begin{equation}
	\left( \frac {3 C} {2} + k \right)^2 = \frac 9 4 C^2 + 3 k C + k^2 > 2 k C + 2 C^2.
\end{equation}

In a similar vein, we now return to the equation for $\rho_i$

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\left( \frac 1 2 (1 - c_i) - k_i \right)^2 &= \frac 1 4 (1 - c_i)^2 - (1- c_i) k_i + k_i^2 \\
	&= \frac 1 4 - \frac {c_i} 2 + \frac {c_i^2} 4 - k_i + c_i k_i + k_i^2 \\
	&\geq \frac 1 4 - \frac {3 c_i} 2 + \frac {c_i^2} 4 - k_i + c_i k_i + k_i^2.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

So both $\rho_i \geq 0$, with equality if and only if $c_i = 0$, which gives the extinction equilibrium as expected.

We also have $1 \geq 1 - c_i - 2 k_i \geq \rho_i$, so these are both valid solutions, for some conditions which we now derive.

We note that as $C = \sum_j A_{ij} N_j \rho_j$, we have the vector equation $\v C = \m A (\v N \otimes \boldsymbol{\rho})$, where $\v C$ is the vector where each element is $C$, and $\otimes$ denotes the component wise product of two vectors. For our metapopulation model, we have no unconnected vertices, and thus no zero rows or columns in $\m A$, we consider only the case where $\m A$ is invertible. There are no simple conditions for the adjacency matrix of a graph to be invertible, so this process is only applicable to those which do have an invertible adjacency matrix. We can then write $\v N \boldsymbol{\rho} = \m A^{-1} \v C = C \m A^{-1} \v 1$, where $\v 1$ is the vector where each element is $1$. We thus let $\m A^{-1} = \v a$, so we have $\rho_i = \frac {C a_i} {N_i}$. Substituting this into our equation for $\rho_i$ gives

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\frac {C a_i} {N_i} &= \frac 1 2 (1 - c_i) - k_i \\
	&\qquad \pm \sqrt{\frac 1 4 - \frac {3 c_i} 2 + \frac {c_i^2} {4} - k_i + k_i c_i + k_i^2} \\
	\iff \left( \frac {C a_i} {N_i} + \frac k {N_i} + \frac C {2 N_i} - \frac 1 2 \right)^2 &= \frac 1 4 - \frac {3C} {2N_i} + \frac {C^2} {4 N_i^2} - \frac k {N_i} + \frac {k C} {N_i^2} + \frac {k^2} {N_i^2} \\
	\iff \frac {C^2 a_i^2} {N_i^2} + \frac {2 C k a_i} {N_i^2} + \frac {C^2 a_i} {N_i^2} - \frac {C a_i} {N_i} &= - \frac C {N_i} \\
	\iff \frac {C^2 a_i^2} {N_i} + \frac {2 C k a_i} {N_i} + \frac {C^2 a_i} {N_i} - C a_i + C &= 0 \\
	\iff \left( \frac {a_i^2} {N_i} + \frac {a_i} {N_i} \right) C^2 + \left( \frac {2 k a_i} {N_i} - a_i + \frac 1 {N_i} \right) C &= 0.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

This has two solutions. Trivially, $C=0$, giving the extinction equilibrium. Or

\begin{equation}
	C = \frac {a_i N_i - 1 - 2 k a_i} {a_i^2 + a_i}.
\end{equation}

We can rearrange this to give a condition on $N_i$

\begin{equation}
	N_i = \frac {a_i^2 C + a_i C + 2 k a_i + 1} {a_i}.
\end{equation}

This is always positive, so after selecting a $C$ we can calculate the necessary $N_i$ for the equilibrium to exist, and then $\rho_i$ will be determined as above. This can obviously only be performed for graphs with an invertible adjacency matrix, as mentioned earlier.

We can now analyse the stability of these equilibria, following the methods set out in \citep{porterDynamicalSystemsNetworks2015}. In their notation, we have

\begin{align}
	f_i(\rho_i) &= k_I \rho_i(t) N_i (1 - \rho_i(t)) - k_S \rho_i(t), \\
	g_{ij}(\rho_i, \rho_j) &= k_I N_i (1 - \rho_i(t)) \rho_j(t).
\end{align}

We likewise also define

\begin{align}
	a_i &:= k_I N_i (1 - 2 \rho_i^*(t)) - k_S, \\
	b_{ij} &:= -k_I N_i \rho_j^*(t), \\
	c_{ij} &:= k_I N_i (1 - \rho_i^*(t)).
\end{align}

Where $\rho_i^*(t)$ are the equilibria defined above. We then define the matrix $\m M$ as in \citep{porterDynamicalSystemsNetworks2015}

\begin{equation}
	M_{ij} = \delta_{ij} \left[ a_i + \sum_k b_{ik} A_{ik} \right] + c_{ij} A_{ij}.
\end{equation}

Letting the eigenvalues of this matrix be $\mu_r$, we then have the usual stability conditions of local asymptotic stability if $\mathrm{Re}(\mu_r) < 0$ for all $r$, instability if any $\mathrm{Re}(\mu_r)> 0$ and more complex stability if some $\mathrm{Re} (\mu_r) = 0$ and none of the eigenvalues have a positive real part.

For the extinction equilibrium, we have

\begin{equation}
	M_{ij} = k_I N_i A_{ij} + \delta_{ij} (k_I N_i - k_S).
\end{equation}

If we now take the populations $N_i = N$ to be the same at every node, we can write $\m M = k_I N \m A + (k_I N - k_S) \m I$. Then, as in \citet{porterDynamicalSystemsNetworks2015}, we have that any eigenvector of $\m A$, $\v w_r$ with eigenvalue $\lambda_r$ satisfies $(k_I N \m A + (k_I N - k_S) \m I) \v w_r = (k_I N \lambda_r + (k_I N - k_S)) \v w_r$ and so any eigenvector of $\m A$ is also an eigenvector of $\m M$. Again an equilibrium here is asymptotically stable if and only if all of the eigenvalues of $\m M$ are negative (as $\m M$ is symmetric here, they are all real). We thus need $k_I N \lambda_r + (k_I N - k_S) < 0$ for all $\lambda_r$. The authors point to \citet{newmanDynamicalSystemsNetworks2018}, where the result is shown that the adjacency matrix must always have both positive and negative eigenvalues. This shows that we must also have $k_I N - k_S < 0$ for stability. It then follows here that $\lambda_r < (k_S - k_I N)/(k_I N)$ for $k_I N > 0$ and $\lambda_r > (k_S - k_I N)/(k_I N)$ for $k_I N < 0$. As we always have $k_I N > 0$, we can discard this last condition. It is clear, however, that if the greatest eigenvalue, $\lambda_1$, satisfies $\lambda_1 < (k_S - k_I N) / (k_I N)$, then so do all the other eigenvalues. This condition actually implies that $k_S / k_I > N$, so we can write that for stability of the extinction equilibrium, we require

\begin{equation}
	N (\lambda_1 + 1) < \frac {k_S} {k_I}.
\end{equation}

We refer to the point at which $k_S / k_I = N (\lambda_1 + 1)$ as the critical ratio. We refer to a ratio of $k_S / k_I$ greater than this as the subcritical case, and a ratio lower than the critical ratio as the supercritical case.

It also demonstrates to us the impact of different graph structures on the disease spread, showing that more connected populations leads to a faster spread of infection in the supercritical case, and a slower recovery in the subcritical case. This may help to advise future epidemic policy. For example, this demonstrates the effectiveness of social distancing measures (which work to decrease the connectivity of communities), but also show the most important factor in controlling infection is to increase the recovery rate relative to the infection rate. This could be done in the real world by decreasing infection (through hand washing, better sanitation, and preventative measures such as barrier contraception for STIs), or through promoting faster recovery (through more effective treatment, increased standards of living, and faster treatment as a result of more widespread testing). All in all, this model provides a great deal of useful insight and further demonstrates the effectiveness of our chemical approach to disease modelling.

\chapter{Networked individuals model}

To give even more depth to the spatial aspect of this model, we can apply it to a networked population where rather than each node representing a smaller population, each node represents an individual in the overall population. This could be thought of in the context of modelling an invididual village or town, with a smaller population than those in the metapopulation model. In this kind of model, the precise network structure chosen has a large impact on the behaviour of the model. We focus on a few kinds. Firstly, the complete graph. This should entirely reproduce the results of the well-mixed model, as every individual is in contact with every other individual. Second, we shall consider some other simple graphs which should inform our expectations for how the graph structure will impact the disease spread. We will also examine how random percolation on the $2$D lattice graph may affect the disease spread characteristics. We will also look at random graphs more closely mimicking social networks, specifically Barabasi-Albert, Watts-Strogatz, and Erdos-Renyi networks, the former being of particular interest due to being a scale-free network. This is a characteristic which is observed to be common to many social networks \citep{McGlohon2011}. The Watts-Strogatz networks demonstrate high clustering, and specifically the small-world property, which are also observed in a variety of real-world networks \citep{McGlohon2011}. These can all be generated easily using the NetworkX package for Python. Finally, we will use the SNAP dataset \citet{snapnets} to analyse the spread of a disease on a network of Facebook users, and see how this network compares to those mentioned earlier.

Adopting the notation from the metapopulation model, we let $\v{I}$ be the vector storing whether a given node is infected or not, by setting $I_i = 1$ if node $i$ is infected, and $I_i = 0$ otherwise. We set $\m{A}$ to be the adjacency matrix for the graph under examination. We also set the infection rate at $k_I$, and the recovery rate at $k_S$. The chemical equations are

\begin{equation}
	\ce{S_i + I_j ->[A_{ij} k_I] 2 I} \qquad \ce{I_i ->[k_S] S_i}.
\end{equation}

The infection is spread according to the following modified Gillespie SSA:

\begin{algorithmic}
	\State $r_1 \gets$ \Call{Rand}{}.
	\State $r_2 \gets$ \Call{Rand}{}.
	\Comment{Get two random numbers uniform in $[0, 1]$}.
	\State $\alpha(t) \gets k_I (\v{1} - \v{I}(t)) \cdot (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t)) + k_S \sum_i I_i(t)$.
	\Comment{Compute the propensity function}.
	\State $\tau \gets \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \log(1/r_1)$.
	\Comment{Compute the time until next reaction}.
	\If {$r_2 < k_I (\v{1} - \v{I}(t)) \cdot (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))$}
		\State find $j$ such that $r_2 \geq \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \sum_{i = 1}^{j-1} k_I (1 - I_i(t)) (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))_i$ and $r_2 < \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \sum_{i = 1}^j (1 - I_i(t)) (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))_i$.
		Set $I_j(t + \tau) = 1$, and $I_i(t + \tau) = I_i(t)$ for all $i \neq j$.
	\Else
		\State $r_3 \gets r_2 - \frac {k_I} {\alpha(t)} (\v{1} - \v{I}(t)) \cdot (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))$.
		\State find $j$ such that $r_3 \geq \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \sum_{i = 1}^{j-1} k_S I_i(t)$ and $r_3 < \frac 1 {\alpha(t)} \sum_{i = 1}^j k_S I_i(t)$.
		Set $I_j(t + \tau) = 0$ and $I_i(t + \tau) = I_i(t)$ for all $i \neq j$.
	\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}

Where $\v{1}$ is the vector where each element is $1$, so that $\v{1} - \v{I}(t)$ is the vector containing a $0$ for each infected node, and a $1$ for each uninfected node. It is then clear that $(\v{1} - \v{I}(t)) * (\m{A} \times \v{I}(t))_i$, where $*$ is the component-wise product, is $1$ if the infection could possibly spread to node $i$, and $0$ if not. The above algorithm therefore carries out a single reaction step.

We then define $p_i(t) = \mathbb{P}(I_i(t) = 1)$ to be the probability that node $i$ is infected at time $t$. Considering a small timestep $\Delta t$ then gives use

\begin{equation}
	p_i(t + \Delta t) = (1 - p_i(t)) k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) \Delta(t) + p_i(t) (1 - k_S \Delta t).
\end{equation}

The first term here is the chance of node $i$ being infected in a time step $\Delta t$, with $(1 - p_i(t))$ being the probability of node $i$ being uninfected at time $t$. The chance of infection is also higher if node $i$ has more neighbours. This is a deliberate choice of the model, to represent most social interactions better, where the majority of individuals any given person interacts with have a similar chance of spreading a disease to them. Particularly for the low-degree networks, representing more difficult to spread diseases, it is reasonable to suggest that the interactions between individuals are roughly comparable. The second term in this expression is simply the probability that node $i$ was already infected, and has not recovered in the considered time step.

Taking the limit as $\Delta t \rightarrow 0$ gives

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\fd {p_i} {t} &= k_I (1 - p_i(t)) \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_S p_i(t) \\
	&= k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - \left( k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) + k_S \right) p_i(t). \label{indvCME}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

This can then be rewritten in vector notation

\begin{equation}
	\fd {\v{p}} {t} = k_I \m{A} \v{p}(t) - k_I (\m{A} \v{p}(t)) \odot \v{p}(t) - k_S \v{p}(t).
\end{equation}

Where $\odot$ is the component-wise vector product.

By setting the average number of infected individuals to $M(t) = \sum_{i} p_i(t)$, we can show the following

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\fd {M} {t} &= \sum_{i} \fd {p_i} {t} \\
	&= \sum_i \left( k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - \left( k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) + k_S \right) p_i(t) \right) \\
	&= \sum_i \left( (1-p_i(t)) k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_S p_i(t) \right) \\
	&= \sum_i (1-p_i(t)) k_I \sum_j A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_S M(t) \\
	&= k_I \sum_{i, j} (1-p_i(t)) A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_S M(t).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

We can take the mean-field limit here, and treat $p_i(t)$ and $p_j(t)$ as independent and identically distributed, giving

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	\fd {M} {t} &= k_I \sum_{ij} A_{ij} p_j(t) - k_I \sum_{ij} A_{ij} p_i(t) p_j(t) - k_S M(t) \\
	&\approx k_I \bar{d(A)} M(t) - k_I \bar{d(A)} \sum_{i} p_i(t)^2 - k_S M(t), 
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $\bar{d(A)}$ is the average degree of the nodes. Then using the identity $V(t) = \sum_{i} p_i(t)^2 - M(t)^2$, we get

\begin{equation}
	\fd {M} {t} \approx k_I \bar{d(A)} M(t) - k_I \bar{d(A)} V(t) - k_I \bar{d(A)} M(t)^2 - k_S M(t).
\end{equation}

This is the same as the equation for the mean in the well-mixed case, with a scaled infection coefficient $k_I \bar{d(A)}$. We can thus suspect that the equilibria of the system will be approximately those of the well-mixed system, with an extinction and endemic equilbria. We can further conjecture that the stability of the endemic equilibria will likewise depend on the coefficients, and so we can estimate the stable regime on a given graph by using the average node degree of the graph, which we will utilise to examine subcritical, transcritical, and supercritical regimes on the different graphs we analyse.

It is difficult to directly analyse this system for an arbitrary network, so we will look at only the complete graph, due to its simple adjacency matrix. For this graph, the original equation reduces to

\begin{equation}
	\fd {M} {t} = k_I (N-1) M(t) - k_I \sum_{i \neq j} p_i p_j - k_S M(t)
\end{equation}

This is highly reminiscent of the well-mixed mean (\ref{SISmixmean}), as we would expect, and the similarity of \ref{fig:SISindvcomp} to \ref{fig:SISmix} is apparent.

We can make a brief attempt to find and analyse some equilibria of these systems, by treating the CME probabilities as a continuous approximation of the system, where we can interpret the probabilities approximately as the amount of time we would expect to see a given node infected for. Setting the derivative in the CME \ref{indvCME} to $0$, we get a rather complex expression for the probabilities. To simplify the situation, we consider the mean-field approximation, where we take all $p_i = p$ to be identically and independently distributed. This gives us

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	0 &= k_I p(t) \sum_j A_{ij} - k_I p^2(t) \sum_j A_{ij} - k_S p(t) \\
	&= p(t) \left( k_I \sum_j A_{ij} - k_I p(t) \sum_j A_{ij} - k_S \right).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

This shows that $p_i = p = 0$ is an equilibrium of the system, as we would expect. The other possibility is given by $p(t) = 1 - \frac {k_S} {k_I \sum_j A_{ij}}$. The requirement that $p(t)$ are all identical requires that $\sum_j A_{ij}$ is the same for all $i$. This is satisfied, for example, by several kinds of tree graph, the complete graph, the cycle graph, and (in the infinite limit) the $2$D lattice. We again see that in the case of the complete graph, we reproduce behaviour very close to that of the well-mixed model, as if we sum over the $p_i$, we get $M = N - \frac {k_S N} {k_I (N-1)}$. Analysing the stability of these equilibria is unfortunately very difficult, as we would be required to find the eigenvalues of an $N \times N$ matrix, where $N$ is in general very large. However, as the behaviour is not dissimilar to the well-mixed and metapopulation models, we could expect to see some kind of critical point at which the extinction equilibrium becomes stable.

These are the Cycle graph, Tadpole graph, Lollipop graph, and Barbell graph. We then move on to some more complex random-generated graphs, these being the Erdos-Renyi, Barabasi-Albert, and Watts-Strogatz. We then simulate on a small selection of percolation-type lattices, with differing percolation coefficients.

These different kinds of random graphs exhibit a variety of interesting properties, but all also have limitations compared to real-life examples of social networks. The Erdos-Renyi model exhibits low clustering, which is not the case with real social networks (if person $A$ knows people $B$ and $C$, then it is generally likely that person $B$ knows person $C$). The Barabasi-Albert model and Watts-Strogatz model do not suffer from this problem, but have their own shortcomings. The Watts-Strogatz model does exhibit high clustering, as well as the small-world property (including short average path lengths, the famous "seven degrees of separation" idea), but is not scale-free, which social networks are thought to be. This is a shortcoming that the Barabasi-Albert model seeks to address, however it too does not demonstrate the small-world property. A further abstraction is made in the percolation-type networks, where every individual can only be connected to a maximum of $4$ others. This is not highly realistic for most diseases, though could be the case for diseases spread only through specific forms of contact (such as STIs or blood-borne diseases), which most individuals only experience with a small number of others. The percolation graphs demonstrate a strong difference in behaviour based on how well-connected the network is as a whole, which may be useful in understanding the spread of diseases such as HIV, HPV, or hepatitis. We also see very clearly from these different simulations that the specifics of a given graph have a very strong impact on the disease characteristics.

Somewhat surprisingly, we find that a critical percolation network is actually the most similar in behaviour to the facebook network. We also once again see that the structure of the networks has a large impact on the exact dynamics of the disease spread, particularly affecting the rate at which the disease propogates across the graph. However, it is apparent that the greatest impact on the overall long-term behaviour is from the exact parameters used, specifically the ratio between infection and recovery rates. We see that in all cases, if the recovery rate is sufficiently high relative to the infection rate, the disease will invariably become extinct. This may be useful to inform public health policy, as it shows that the most effective means to reduce the impact of an epidemic is to lower the rate of infection (for example, through better hand washing and sanitation), or to increase the rate of recovery (by developing better treatments, or screening greater numbers of people to be able to start treatment earlier).
